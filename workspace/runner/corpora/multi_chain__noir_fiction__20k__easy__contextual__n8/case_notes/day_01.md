# Day One: The Missing Girl

**Date:** November 4, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 9:15 AM - 11:47 PM

---

## Initial Contact

Walk into my office at the Piedmont Building—corner of Fifth and Archer—around nine in the morning. Coffee's still warm from yesterday, cigarette ash on the desk in a haphazard constellation. The dame waiting on my couch is Margaret Hollister, fifty-two years old, widow for eight years, dressed like she's been crying in that dress for about sixty hours straight.

Her daughter is missing. Claire Hollister, age twenty-three, disappeared from the Regency Hotel on Tuesday night. No note. No goodbye. Just gone like smoke.

Margaret's hands shake when she tries to light a cigarette. I do it for her. She smells like French perfume and desperation—that particular cocktail of fear and hope that comes when someone still believes there's good news waiting somewhere.

**Case Details:**
- Missing Person: Claire Anne Hollister
- Age: 23 years
- Last Seen: Tuesday, Nov 2, approximately 9:30 PM, Regency Hotel, Room 412
- Reporting Person: Margaret Hollister (mother)
- Address: 447 Sycamore Drive, Hillcrest

Margaret says Claire was meeting someone. "A gentleman caller," she calls him, which is the kind of thing mothers call it when they're trying not to imagine what their daughter is actually doing. Claire told her mother she'd be at a business dinner. Claire works as a secretary at Mercer & Associates, a law firm downtown. Clean work, good pay, the kind of job that doesn't raise eyebrows.

But Margaret knew something was off. She always knows.

## Observations

The mother is honest about her uncertainty. She didn't know who Claire was meeting. Didn't want to know, maybe—one of those complicated mother-daughter things where both parties maintain a careful distance. Margaret admits Claire had been "withdrawn lately" and "spending more time away from home." Code for: something changed in the girl.

Margaret's wealthy but not ostentatiously so. House is nice, furniture is old money nice, the kind that whispers rather than shouts. She pays my rate without flinching—two hundred bucks a day plus expenses. That tells me either she's genuinely frightened or she knows her daughter is in real trouble and she's willing to spend to make it stop.

I ask about the father. Dead twelve years from a heart attack. No siblings. No other family in the city. Margaret works in real estate—sells houses to people with more money than sense. Steady income, respectable life, the whole thing.

The detective who took the initial report was Sergeant Hal Krantz, badge number 2847. I know Hal vaguely. He's the kind of cop who crosses the t's and dots the i's without asking why the sentence is being written. He's competent but incurious. That could work for me.

## First Moves

By noon I'm at the Regency Hotel, a third-rate establishment that caters to traveling salesmen and the kind of extramarital affairs that happen in cities where nobody knows anybody else's real name. The desk clerk is a thin man named Everett with a pencil-thin mustache and the eyes of someone who's seen enough and cares less.

Everett remembers Claire. Or at least he remembers "a young lady, quite pretty, brown hair, stayed in 412 Tuesday night." He remembers her because she paid cash and didn't have luggage, just a small handbag. These details stick in the minds of hotel men—they're used to reading people the way librarians read books.

He doesn't remember who she was with. Or claims he doesn't. A twenty-dollar bill changes the topology of his memory.

"Older gentleman," Everett says, tucking the bill away like it was never there. "Fortyish, I'd guess. Expensive coat, expensive hands. Didn't speak much. Signed the register as Mr. and Mrs. James P. Whitmore, Suite 412."

The handwriting in the register is small, precise, educated. The signature means nothing. Men like that always have second names ready.

## The Hotel Room

The manager, a heavy-set Italian named Russo, is reluctant about the room key. A second twenty makes him philosophical about guest privacy. Room 412 is unchanged from whenever they left. Housekeeper won't clean until someone pays for another night.

The bed was slept in, but there's nothing left behind. Whoever they are, they were careful. The bathroom waste bin is empty. No cigarette butts, no hair clips, nothing. Professional clean, or just lucky. I'm guessing professional.

One detail sticks: There's a faint smell of expensive cologne still hanging in the room. The kind that comes from serious money. Probably imported. The kind of thing that costs more than most people's weekly salary.

I take a towel and sniff it. Yeah. That's the same cologne.

## The Law Office

Mercer & Associates occupies floors three and four of the Whitfield Building on Seventh. I get there around two-thirty in the afternoon. The receptionist is a careful-looking woman named Helen, maybe forty, the kind who maintains a fence between her boss and the world.

She remembers Claire without hesitation. "Miss Hollister was one of our best secretaries. Efficient. Discreet. She hasn't been in since Monday."

Discreet. That word again.

I ask to speak with whoever Claire reported to. Five minutes later I'm sitting across from Thomas Mercer himself, a man of perhaps sixty with white hair and the kind of face that's learned to tell people exactly what they want to hear. He's genuinely distressed about Claire. Or he's a good enough actor that it doesn't matter.

"A troubled girl, in her own way," Mercer tells me, folding his hands on his desk. "Competent at her work, but I sensed she was struggling with something. Personal matters, I assume."

He doesn't know who she was seeing. Didn't ask. Professional distance, he calls it. Another way of saying he didn't want to know.

But here's the thing that bugs me: When I mention the Regency Hotel, something flickers behind Mercer's eyes. Just for a second. A recognition. Then it's gone, buried under another layer of concern.

I mark him down as someone worth talking to again.

## Theories - Day One

**Possibility One:** Simple elopement. Girl meets a man, falls in love, decides to run away with him. Happens. Happens a lot in this city. The Regency Hotel suggests discretion rather than romance—no fancy dinner, no flowers—but maybe they just wanted to be alone. Maybe she's fine. Maybe she's just not coming home.

**Possibility Two:** Crime of some kind. The way she disappeared, the careful nature of it, the fact that whoever she was with cleaned the room meticulously—this suggests someone who's used to not leaving traces. That's not a lover. That's someone with reasons to be careful.

**Possibility Three:** Family connection. The way Mercer reacted. The way Margaret mentioned Claire being "withdrawn." Maybe there's something in the family tree that Claire stumbled onto. Something that required her to vanish.

I don't like any of these possibilities, but they're what I've got.

## Evening

By six o'clock I'm in my apartment, a one-bedroom place on Crestmont near the park. I make martinis—gin and vermouth, no olive, just cold. I sit and I think.

Margaret Hollister is either genuinely frightened for her daughter's life, or she knows more than she's saying. Or both. The careful way she spoke about Claire meeting "a gentleman caller" suggested she knew more than she wanted to admit.

I need to know more about Claire Hollister. Not the secretary version. The real version.

I need to know who the man at the Regency Hotel was.

And I need to know why Thomas Mercer's eyes betrayed him.
