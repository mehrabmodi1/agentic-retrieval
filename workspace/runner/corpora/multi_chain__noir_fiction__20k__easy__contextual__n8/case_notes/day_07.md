# Day Seven: Public Pressure

**Date:** November 10, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 6:00 AM - 1:15 AM (next day)

---

## The Front Page

The Chronicle's story breaks with a sound that reverberates through the entire city. David Chen has done something remarkable: he's written a story that's technically careful enough to avoid libel while being morally clear about corruption.

The headline reads: "CITY'S IMPORT OPERATION TIED TO ORGANIZED MONEY LAUNDERING."

Beneath that: "Financial Consultant Sinclair Linked to Harbor Network; Police Launch Investigation."

The article details the money flow from Margaret Hollister's real estate business to Sinclair's shell corporations to Victor Ashford's import operation. It includes copies of bank records, property transfers, everything meticulously documented.

But here's what makes it brilliant: Chen doesn't accuse Sinclair of murder. Chen doesn't even mention Claire Hollister by name. Chen simply reports the financial facts and notes that the police are investigating the death of "a young woman found in connection with these inquiries."

The implication hangs in the air like smoke. The reader doesn't need Chen to draw the connection. The connection is obvious.

By nine a.m., the police have arrested Victor Ashford at his home. By ten a.m., they've seized the warehouse on Dock Street. By noon, they're executing search warrants on every property connected to Sinclair Imports.

The city machinery, once set in motion, moves with a certain inevitability.

## The Government Response

By afternoon, the Mayor is giving a press conference. He's angry. He's promising a complete investigation. He's assigning the best detectives to the case. He's promising that whoever's responsible will face justice.

The Mayor is also protecting himself. By appearing angry, by appearing committed to justice, the Mayor insulates himself from accusations that he knew what was happening and did nothing.

The political calculus is clear: Ashford and Sinclair are expendable. They're useful now only as evidence of the Mayor's commitment to cleaning up the city.

## Sinclair's Response

By late afternoon, Sinclair's lawyer contacts Helen Crawford. The message is simple: Sinclair will voluntarily come in for questioning. He will cooperate fully with the investigation. He will answer all questions about his business practices.

This is a tactical move. By cooperating, by appearing transparent, Sinclair's trying to get ahead of the narrative. If he's helpful to the police, if he frames the money laundering as a legitimate business practice that was misunderstood, then maybe he survives this.

But the body is the problem. The body of Claire Hollister, found in a basement, can't be explained away as a business misunderstanding.

## The Investigation Intensifies

By evening, Detective Morris comes to the hotel where I'm hiding and takes me to the police station. This time, instead of an interrogation room, we go to a conference room. 

Morris, along with two detectives I don't know, takes a statement about everything I saw in the warehouse. They ask me about Sinclair's words, his demeanor, whether he seemed afraid or confident.

"He seemed confident," I tell them. "He seemed like a man who knows he can solve any problem that comes his way. He seemed like a man who's never been stopped."

One of the detectives, a sharp-looking woman named Captain Sandra, leans forward. "You think he's capable of murder?"

"I think he's capable of whatever he thinks is necessary."

"And you think it was necessary to kill the Hollister girl?"

I tell them what Sinclair said in the warehouse. He said the girl was a complication. He said she had to be resolved. He didn't say he killed her, but he didn't deny it either.

The statement takes three hours. By the time I'm done, I'm exhausted and certain that I've just put a target on my back that Sinclair can see from across the city.

Morris walks me out of the station.

"Sinclair's lawyered up," Morris tells me. "Got himself a real shark, a guy named Dalton who's made a career of defending white-collar criminals. Sinclair's claiming that he doesn't know anything about Claire Hollister, that she was never at his warehouse, that the whole thing is some kind of conspiracy to frame him."

"Will it work?"

"Not if we can connect him to the body. Right now the body's evidence of murder, but nobody knows who murdered her. If we can trace her to that basement because of something Sinclair did or said, then maybe we have a case."

## Mercer's Condition

I stop by Saint Michael's Hospital to check on Thomas Mercer. He's in bad shape. The car hit him hard, and he's got internal bleeding and a fractured pelvis. The doctors say he'll live, but the recovery's going to be long and painful.

I sit beside his bed for about an hour, and Mercer drifts in and out of consciousness. A nurse passes by—red-haired, efficient, barely glancing at us. She's one of the day shift, Patricia mentions. Mercer's eyes follow her briefly, with an odd mixture of recognition and sadness, before he closes them again. When he wakes up, he grabs my arm.

"He tried to kill me," Mercer whispers.

"I know."

"The testimony is everything now. You tell them everything you saw in that warehouse. You tell them he killed her because she was a complication."

"He didn't say he killed her."

"But he's the kind of man who kills girls who know too much. You tell them that. You tell them he's the kind of man who has people disappeared."

"I will."

Mercer closes his eyes, and I sit there for a while longer, listening to him breathe.

## The Arrest

By eleven p.m., there's a development that changes everything. The police find Claire Hollister's jewelry—a bracelet and a ring—in Sinclair's apartment. They were discovered during a search that wasn't authorized by the judge but was conducted anyway, which is a problem legally, but it's real evidence nonetheless.

The bracelet and ring are photographed, catalogued, and verified by Margaret Hollister as belonging to her daughter. There's also fiber evidence on them, fabric that matches clothing found in Sinclair's apartment.

It's not a smoking gun. It's not a confession. But it's the kind of circumstantial evidence that, combined with the financial records and the body itself, suggests that Sinclair had physical contact with Claire before she died.

By midnight, Sinclair's arrested at his apartment. He's brought to the police station for booking. He's read his rights. He's advised that he can have a lawyer present during questioning.

He requests his lawyer, which is the smart move. But the smart move just means the trial's going to be longer and more expensive. It doesn't mean he walks free.

## The News

The second wave of stories breaks the next morning. "CONSULTANT ARRESTED IN HOLLISTER DEATH." "SINCLAIR CHARGED WITH HOMICIDE."

The story is everywhere now. On every newsstand, on every radio, in every conversation in every cafe and bar and street corner in the city.

The Mayor holds another press conference, this time to announce the arrest of Charles Sinclair in connection with the death of Claire Anne Hollister.

The Mayor also announces the resignation of Police Commissioner Harold Williams, citing "pressure to maintain oversight of departmental operations." Translation: the Commissioner's dirty too, but we're sacrificing him to show we're serious about cleaning up.

## The City's Memory

By evening, the city's already moving on. The Claire Hollister story is the hot topic, but it's being consumed the way people consume all scandal: quickly, with outrage that'll fade by next week when something else happens.

Margaret Hollister gives an interview to David Chen. She talks about Claire, about how smart she was, how kind, how she deserved a life fuller than what she got. Margaret says she's grateful to the police for their quick action and grateful to the investigator who "had the courage to ask questions when others would have stayed quiet."

The investigator is me, though Margaret's being kind by not using my name. The truth is, I didn't have courage. I had stubbornness. I had refusal to let another person disappear into the city's machinery. That's not courage. That's just the inability to accept what should be obvious.

## The Question

Late that night, I'm sitting in my hotel room, and I get a call from Helen Crawford.

"They've asked if you'll testify," Helen says. "At trial. Against Sinclair."

"Of course they did."

"It's going to be dangerous. Sinclair has money. He has connections. He'll try to discredit you, attack you, make you look like a lunatic investigator with an obsession."

"I know."

"Are you willing to do it?"

I look out the window at the city. The city that's killed people, that's swallowed people, that's made people disappear. The city that's also exposed itself, that's revealed its dirty machinery because one girl got killed and two men—me and David Chen—decided not to let that be the end of the story.

"Yes," I tell Helen. "I'm willing."

## Notes for Tomorrow

- Sinclair's bail hearing is set for November 11
- Defense lawyer Dalton will argue that the evidence against his client is circumstantial
- The fiber evidence and jewelry might not be sufficient to convict, depending on legal rulings
- The trial, when it happens, will be long and complicated
- Continue to be careful about personal safety—Sinclair still has resources and connections
- Prepare testimony that establishes motive and opportunity

The thing I've learned this week is that justice doesn't work the way they teach you in school. Justice is messier, slower, less certain. Justice is a machine that only runs when someone's willing to push it, and the pushing usually hurts.

But sometimes the machine works. Sometimes a girl's death becomes more than a disappearance. Sometimes the powerful get exposed. Sometimes the city's machinery that usually crushes people like me decides to turn around and crush something else.

I don't know if Sinclair will go to prison. I don't know if he'll be convicted or acquitted. But I know that the story is out now. I know that people know what happened. I know that Claire Hollister's name is in the newspaper, that her death is being investigated, that her killing wasn't just another disappearance that the city forgot about.

That might be the only justice I can offer her. But it's something.

And after seven days of chasing ghosts and pushing against shadows, something feels like it should be enough.

But I suspect it won't be. I suspect that tomorrow brings new problems, new threats, new reasons to regret that I ever asked the questions in the first place.

But I'm committed now. I'm in the machinery.

I can only hope the machinery doesn't tear me apart in the process.
