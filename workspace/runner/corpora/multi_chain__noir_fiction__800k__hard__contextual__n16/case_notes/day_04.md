# Day Four: Unraveling the Web

**Date:** November 7, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 7:15 AM - 3:45 AM (next day)

---

## The Call to Eddie

Eddie picks up the phone on the second ring. He knows what I want before I ask.

"You're playing with fire," Eddie says, which is his way of confirming he can help.

I give him the license plate number. He tells me to call back in an hour.

While I wait, I shower, eat, try to pretend that I'm not about to take a step that can't be walked back. The thing about cases is that they have a momentum of their own. Once you start pulling threads, you have to keep pulling. The alternative is to let them dangle, which means they'll just wrap around you and strangle you.

Eddie calls back exactly fifty-eight minutes later.

"The car is registered to a company called Sinclair Holdings Limited, address listed as a P.O. box in the financial district. Owner of record is someone named Charles J. Sinclair. That's the name on the paperwork. But Eddie, and listen to me careful here—Charles J. Sinclair is the kind of person that exists on paper only."

"What do you mean, exists on paper?"

"I mean that the address is real, the company is real, the car is real. But the man? The man is fiction. I checked with the IRS. The company exists, files taxes, everything legitimate on the surface. But nobody I talked to has ever actually seen Charles J. Sinclair in person." This ghost of a man had left something concrete behind—a business card—which Patricia found at the hospital when the injured kid was brought in. That card would become physical evidence of Sinclair's existence, even if Sinclair himself seemed to exist only in records.

That's interesting. That's the kind of interesting that keeps you up at night.

## Finding Vincent

I spend the morning doing what I should have done earlier: checking the hospitals, the emergency rooms, anywhere a broken-up kid might get stitched together.

Third stop, Saint Michael's Hospital in the South End, a clerk named Patricia who remembers Vincent Castellano being brought in Wednesday morning. Multiple contusions, two broken ribs, severe contusion to the head. According to hospital records, he was in their facility for exactly three years prior to this incident as well. Admitted under the name James Patterson. The coincidence bothers me until Patricia mentions that the man who picked him up left a business card that fell out of his pocket—"Charles Sinclair - Private Consulting Services"—with an address that traces directly back to those Meridian Holdings shell corporations Margaret was selling property to.

Patricia thinks that's funny, the fake name. "Like anyone couldn't figure out who he was."

Vincent's listed as being released Thursday morning, but Patricia doesn't think he was actually released.

"What do you mean?" I ask.

"I mean that I helped wheel him to the discharge area, but a man was waiting for him. Well-dressed man, didn't say much. He helped Vincent into a car and they drove off. The discharge paperwork was signed, but nobody actually watched him walk out the front door."

So Vincent's still in someone's custody. Probably Sinclair's. Which raises the question of whether Vincent is a prisoner or something else.

## Margaret's Truth

I drive to Margaret's house. The real house, not the office. It's a nice place, the kind that costs serious money. The kind that's paid for with serious money.

Margaret answers the door wearing a bathrobe at eleven in the morning. She's fallen apart. Actually fallen apart this time. Not just the appearance of falling apart, but the actual dissolution of a person.

"I know what happened to her," I tell Margaret without preamble.

Margaret sits down on her expensive couch and doesn't say anything.

"She's dead," I continue. "She was killed Wednesday night at a warehouse in the harbor district. The man responsible is someone called Sinclair, though that's probably not his real name. The crime involved Vincent Castellano, who's probably also dead by now or soon will be. And the money from your real estate business is connected to the people who killed her."

Margaret stares at nothing.

"I need you to tell me the truth," I say. "Everything. Who approached you? When did the money laundering start? What was Claire involved in?"

Margaret's voice, when she speaks, is very small.

"It started five years ago. A man came to my office and offered to make me very rich. All I had to do was buy certain properties and sell them to certain buyers. He provided the money, I provided the legitimacy of the transaction. Nobody had to know that the money came from illegal sources. Everything stayed clean on the surface."

"This man," I ask. "Was it Sinclair?"

"No. This was someone else. Someone older, someone with real power. He said his name was Castellano. I assumed he was related to the family I was laundering for, but he said no, that was just a coincidence. He wanted to use the Castellano network to move money, but he wasn't one of them."

"And Claire?"

"Claire found out. She was smart, my daughter. She was better at accounts than I was, and she was going through my files one night, wondering about the math. She figured it out. She asked me about it, and I told her the truth."

"What did she do?"

"She wanted to go to the police. I told her that going to the police would get us both killed. I told her that some people don't go to jail, they just disappear. I told her to stay quiet and everything would be fine. And then she started seeing Vincent Castellano, and I thought maybe they could run away together, live somewhere else, get out of it."

Margaret's face crumples. "I was so stupid. I thought if she was away from the city, away from all of this, she'd be safe. But someone knew. Someone figured out that she knew. And they decided she needed to be dealt with before she became a problem."

"The person who approached you originally," I say. "The one claiming to be Castellano. What did he look like?"

Margaret gives me a description that matches, word for word, the description of the man who's been visiting me in the night. The man called Sinclair.

## The Money Trail

I spend the afternoon at the Public Library, going through financial records that are theoretically public but actually hidden in plain sight by bureaucratic complexity. Margaret's real estate company has moved a lot of money. Millions of dollars over five years.

All of it connected to shell companies. All of it traced back to properties in the harbor district. All of it associated with a name that changes every few transactions but always has a P.O. box address.

I finally find what I'm looking for in an old newspaper article from three years ago. A story about a shipping magnate named Victor Ashford. Ashford supposedly controls most of the import business coming into the city, though the article is careful to note that this is merely alleged.

But the article mentions someone working with Ashford: a man named Charles Sinclair, described as Ashford's "financial advisor and representative."

That's the name. Charles Sinclair. The same name on the car registration. The same man who's been visiting me and threatening me and moving girls into warehouses.

## The Harbor Again

By four in the afternoon I'm back at the waterfront, looking for Paulie. I find him at the Anchor, nursing a beer and looking nervous.

"I need information," I tell him. "About Victor Ashford."

Paulie goes white. Not pale. White. Like the blood just drained out of his face.

"No," Paulie says. "No, no, no. I'm not talking about Ashford. I like being alive."

"Someone's already killed at least one person," I tell him. "A girl named Claire Hollister. She was innocent. Wrong place, wrong time, knew the wrong thing. I can't bring her back. But I can maybe stop it from happening again."

Paulie looks around like Ashford might be listening from behind the bar.

"Ashford's not the boss," Paulie whispers. "Ashford's what you see. The real power is someone else. Someone who doesn't show up in newspapers or anywhere public. Someone who's been running this city for longer than I've been alive and probably longer than that."

"Who?"

"I don't know. But I know that Sinclair works for him. And I know that Sinclair is the kind of man who makes people disappear. Not murders them messy where there's blood and cops asking questions. He makes them go away clean. No bodies. No records. Just gone."

"How many people?"

Paulie shakes his head. "Not enough to make a pattern. Not enough for anyone to notice. But enough. Over the years, enough."

## The Visitor Again

I'm not surprised when I get home and find Mr. Sinclair sitting in my apartment. The door was locked, but I don't think that's ever stopped a man like him.

"You've been busy," Sinclair says, not moving from my chair. "I gave you a warning. You didn't take it."

"The girl is dead," I say. It's not a question.

"The girl was a complication. She had to be resolved."

"And Vincent Castellano?"

"Is being taken care of. He'll disappear, just like the girl. In a few weeks, nobody will even remember they existed."

I move to the window, trying to think. Trying to figure out what I'm supposed to do with a man like this sitting in my apartment, confident that he can kill me and that nobody will care.

"Why are you telling me this?" I ask.

"Because I want you to understand the situation. I want you to understand that you're not investigating a crime. You're investigating something that doesn't exist, or will be made not to exist very shortly. The girl is gone. The Castellano family has learned a lesson about keeping their children on a leash. And you need to decide whether you want to be part of the solution or part of the problem."

"You're offering me a choice."

"I'm offering you an exit. Take the money Margaret Hollister paid you, consider the case closed, and move on with your life. Tell people that the girl ran away with her boyfriend. Simple story. Easy to believe. And you get to keep breathing."

I turn to look at Sinclair. He's relaxed in my chair, completely at ease, the kind of man who's gotten what he wanted for so long that he can't imagine not getting it.

"What if I don't take the exit?"

"Then you become a complication. And complications get resolved the same way the girl did."

"Someone has to know," I say. "Someone has to remember that Claire Hollister existed. Someone has to be willing to say what happened. The moral choice you're forcing—silence for safety, or truth for death—that's not a legitimate choice. That's coercion masquerading as an offer."

Sinclair stands up. "No they don't. That's the secret they never tell you in detective novels. Nobody has to know. People disappear every day in this city. Thousands of them. They just stop existing, and life goes on. By next month, nobody will remember the girl's name. By next year, it won't even be a story."

He walks toward the door, then pauses. "You have until tomorrow night to accept my offer. After that, I stop making offers."

When he's gone, I stand in my apartment for a long time, trying to figure out what I'm going to do with the knowledge that I'm supposed to forget.

## The Client

Around midnight, I call Margaret.

"They're offering me a deal," I tell her. "Take the money, forget about Claire, move on. That's what's happening. That's what always happens."

Margaret doesn't say anything for a long moment.

"I don't want to forget about her," Margaret finally says. "But I also don't want you to die because of her."

"I know."

"So what are you going to do?"

I don't have an answer to that. I'm a private investigator in a city where private information is for sale and public information is fiction. I'm a man with knowledge of a crime that nobody cares about because the person who committed it is too powerful to touch.

I'm exactly the kind of man that people like Sinclair count on to stay quiet.

## Notes for Tomorrow

- Verify Victor Ashford connection to the harbor district operations
- Find evidence linking Sinclair to Claire Hollister's disappearance (body, witnesses, anything)
- Determine the identity of the person above Sinclair and Ashford
- Decide: take the exit or go deeper
- Consider whether there's any way to win this case, or whether it's already lost

The thing I'm learning about this city is that the law is just theater. The real power operates behind closed curtains, moving money, removing obstacles, making sure that nothing disrupts the show.

The question now is whether I'm willing to be just another obstacle to be removed, or whether I have the courage to actually do something about it.

Looking at my apartment, looking at the city through my window, I'm not sure I have that courage.

But I'm going to find out.
