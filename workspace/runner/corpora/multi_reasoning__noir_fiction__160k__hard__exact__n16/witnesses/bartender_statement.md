
"Not right then. But when I delivered his second drink—he'd finally moved to the one he'd ordered earlier—she paid her tab and walked over. I couldn't hear much. The jukebox was playing loud. But I saw her lean in close, saw her lips moving. The kid's face went white. I mean, I've seen men get bad news in this place. I've poured drinks for guys who just lost their fortunes, their wives, their freedom. But I'd never seen a face drain of color like that. Like someone had just told him the war wasn't over, it was just starting."

"Did they leave together?"

Deke shook his head slowly. "That's the thing. She took his hand—quick gesture, like she was afraid someone might see—squeezed it, and left. Just like that. Walked out like she'd never been there. The kid sat there for another twenty minutes, didn't move, didn't drink anything else. Then he left too. Didn't tip. First time in maybe six months he didn't leave a tip."

"You ever see him with anyone else? Any regular companions?"

"Once. Maybe two weeks ago. There was an older man, maybe late fifties, heavy-set type with white hair and the kind of face that'd probably been handsome twenty years ago before whatever was eating him at the inside did its work. They sat together, didn't talk much. But I noticed the old guy slid something across the table—looked like an envelope. White envelope. The kid put it in his jacket without looking at it. Like he'd been expecting it but didn't want to acknowledge the expectation."

Morello tapped ash into the tray I'd placed on the bar without thinking. "Anything else unusual about their interaction?"

"Yeah, actually." Deke's pale eyes fixed on something in the middle distance, seeing something that happened ten days earlier. "When the old guy left, he put his hand on the kid's shoulder. There was something in the gesture—something almost paternal, you know? But also something like a warning. Like a man putting his hand on a horse's neck before the race, trying to tell it to run but also knowing the horse might not come back."

"He leaned down and said something before he took his hand away. Bar was quiet that night, and I caught a few words clear enough—'the Starlight Motor Inn, room fourteen, the key's under the mat.' Said it like giving directions, real matter-of-fact. The kid nodded once, didn't write it down. The kind of thing a man memorizes because he knows he'll need it."

"The envelope. Did you ever see the kid open it?"

"Didn't stick around to find out. I've learned that the less I know about what happens in the corners and the booths, the longer I keep my liquor license and my teeth." Deke set down the glass he'd been polishing. It caught the light, gleamed like a crystal ball that sees everything but tells nothing. "But I'll tell you something I know about people, Detective. The envelope had the kind of weight to it—the feel of documents, maybe currency. Thick paper, expensive. Not something you stuff with a single note. Whatever was in there, it was meant to set the kid up for something significant."

"The redhead," Morello said. "You ever see her before?"

"Once. Just once. Two months ago, maybe longer. She came in with a different guy—a suit, the expensive kind that costs more than I make in a month. They had champagne. Real champagne, not the rot-gut stuff we pass off to the tourists. They talked for maybe an hour, and then she cried. Just a single tear running down her cheek, but it was the kind of tear that means something got broken that can't be fixed. The guy left money on the table and walked out. She stayed for another drink, alone, staring at nothing."

"You get a name? Anything?"

Deke smiled sadly. "I learned a long time ago not to ask names. Names attach people to places, and places attached to people get found when they shouldn't be found. I keep their secrets because their secrets keep them coming back. That's how this business works."

Morello stubbed out his cigarette and ordered a water. "Anyone threatening the kid? Anyone who seemed to want him gone?"
