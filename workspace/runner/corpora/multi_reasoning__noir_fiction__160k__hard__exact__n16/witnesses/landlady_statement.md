
"Three weeks ago. Maybe two weeks and five days. I remember because it was the day before I ordered new carpet for the downstairs hallway, and the man, he looked like he would notice the carpet. Like he would file away that we have worn burgundy carpet, so if he needed to report on the building to someone, he could say exactly what it looks like."

"Did Mr. Harvey seem different after this man's visit?"

Eva's hands tightened on her ledger. "He stopped pacing at night. Or maybe he paced quieter. And there was something in his eyes the next time I saw him—he came to pay rent, he always comes in person—something that said he had made a decision about something. A big decision. The kind that changes your life."

"When did you last see him?"

"October 29th. He brings the rent check, and he seems lighter, like a weight has been removed. We speak about the weather—it is turning cold—and he says he might spend Thanksgiving with family. Family! In three years, he has never mentioned family. I don't even know if he has family. But that day he mentions it like he is making a promise to himself. He also requested something unusual—asked if I could have the apartment's front door lock changed to a deadbolt within the week. He said for security. Then he leaves."

"And you haven't seen him since?"

"No. But I begin to worry when he does not come out, not for three days, not for four. So on the fourth day, I go up to the apartment. I have a master key—for emergencies, in case someone is sick, has died, has become a danger to themselves. I knock first, loud. No answer. I use the key. The apartment, it is empty. His clothes are gone. His furniture, most of it. He left behind only what could not be carried—the old sofa that was here when he moved in, the kitchenette, things like that. But his personal things, his papers, his life—it is all gone."

"Did he leave any forwarding address?"

"Nothing. He just... vanishes. Like he was never here. But I know he was here because I have the rent checks, and I have the memory of a man who was quiet and careful and afraid. Those things, you cannot erase."

Detective Chen closed her notebook. "Did you ever see him with a young woman? A redhead?"

Eva shook her head. "I would have noticed. In this building, we do not see many beautiful young women visiting one young man's apartment. It would have been unusual. No, Mr. Harvey, he lived alone. Completely alone. That much I am certain of."

"One more question," Chen said. "Do you think he left because he wanted to, or because he had to?"

Eva was quiet for a long moment. She looked out the window at the gray street below, where people moved like ghosts through the afternoon. "A man pays his rent on time for three years, and then he disappears. He never complains, never asks for anything, except to change a lock. He mentions family he has never spoken of before. And then he is gone." She turned back to Chen. "That is not a man who wanted to leave. That is a man who was running. From what, I do not know. But he was running very fast, and he was running from something very serious."
