# Confidential Informant Interview
## Source: "Victor" (Identity Withheld)

**Date:** November 13, 1947  
**Time:** 11:47 PM  
**Location:** Undisclosed, Various Checkpoints  
**Interviewer:** Detective Lieutenant William Halloway  
**Status:** Protected Informant, Criminal History Waived Under Agreement

---

The interview took place in the basement parking structure of an abandoned warehouse, the kind of place where sound dies before it travels and where the only witnesses are the shadows that concrete throws. The informant—code-named Victor for purposes of the report—arrived via taxi, circled the block twice, and entered through a service entrance he seemed to know well. He was nervous, which made Detective Halloway's job easier. Nervous informants talked. Comfortable informants calculated.

"You're going to want to know about the Kaslan operation," Victor said without preamble. He was lean, fifties maybe, with the kind of weathered skin that suggested a life spent outdoors or in environments where the sun was the least dangerous thing present. "That's where your boy Harvey comes in."

"Tell me about it."

"Kaslan is—or was—a distribution network. High-end merchandise, if you know what I mean. Art, antiquities, items that can't be moved through normal channels because of their provenance. About four years ago, Kaslan figured out that there was a market for moving stolen items internationally. You steal a painting in New York, you move it to Toronto, then Montreal, then Havana, then eventually it ends up in Buenos Aires or Mexico City, and nobody can trace its original ownership because it's been through so many hands and jurisdictions."

"And Harvey was involved in this?"

"Harvey was a facilitator. He worked in shipping—he had legitimate credentials and access to dock facilities. But he was also young and smart and probably more conscience-stricken than was good for him. He helped move pieces for maybe eighteen months. Then something happened."

"What kind of something?"

Victor lit a cigarette though there was no ashtray and no indication that Halloway was going to tolerate it. Halloway didn't stop him. "There was a piece. A painting, fifteenth century, supposedly. It came through the network about six months ago. It was valuable—we're talking quarter-million, maybe more. But here's the thing: it wasn't stolen from a museum or a private collector. It was stolen from the widow of a man named Carl Vesper."

Halloway knew that name. Everyone in homicide knew that name. "The banker. The one who was killed in his study five years ago."

"Exactly. Vesper had owned the painting for thirty years. His widow had no idea it was valuable—it was just a painting her husband liked, hanging in his office. But when Kaslan learned about it, he wanted it. The provenance was perfect—an old family estate, documented sale, all clean. It could be moved legitimately through the legal market once it was positioned correctly."

"But something went wrong."

"Somebody developed a conscience," Victor said. "Somebody looked at that painting and realized it had been stolen from a widow whose husband was murdered, and they couldn't live with themselves moving stolen goods that were connected to an unsolved murder. That somebody was Harvey."

"What did he do?"

"He went to an attorney. Guy named Marchetti. Marchetti specializes in people who want to do the right thing but have been doing the wrong thing and need legal cover. Harvey wanted to return the painting, but he was terrified about what would happen to him if he did. Kaslan doesn't take kindly to loyalty that's based on conscience rather than fear."

"Did Marchetti help him?"

"According to my sources—and I have sources in places you don't want to know about—Marchetti advised him to cooperate with law enforcement. Provide information about the Kaslan operation in exchange for immunity or witness protection. It's the only way to safely return the painting and dissolve his connection to the network."

"But Harvey disappeared instead of going to law enforcement."

"Which means Kaslan found out about the betrayal before Harvey could formalize it." Victor finished his cigarette and did something surprising: he looked remorseful. "Harvey's a good kid. Or was. He made a stupid mistake getting involved with Kaslan in the first place, but he tried to correct it. That kind of moral courage gets you killed in this business."

"Do you think he's dead?"

Victor didn't answer for a moment. The parking structure was quiet except for the distant sound of traffic and the constant drip of moisture from pipes overhead. "I think he's either dead or he's running. And if he's running, he's running scared, because Kaslan has a very long reach and very long memory."

"Who's Kaslan? What's his real name?"

"As far as I know, nobody knows Kaslan's real name. He operates through intermediaries. There's a man named Silas Gregg who was his field operative—the one who coordinated with Harvey and the other facilitators. Gregg is probably the person who first realized Harvey was planning to betray the operation. If Harvey ran, Gregg would be the one sent to stop him."

"Describe Gregg."

"White hair, silver. Fifty-five, sixty years old. Heavy-set, but he moves carefully, like he's aware of his body's limitations. Military background—you can tell by the way he carries himself. Formal manner. He'd be the kind of man who wears expensive suits and drinks expensive liquor but who would kill you without hesitation if you represented a business problem."

"One more thing about Gregg," Victor added. "He wears a signet ring on his right hand. Old gold, heavy, the kind of piece that suggests family money or a claim to it. The crest has been polished so smooth you can see the shape of the thing but not the design anymore. I only bring it up because a man like Gregg doesn't wear jewelry for sentiment. He wears it so the right people recognize him without him having to say a word."

Halloway's hand was already writing the description down, but it matched the descriptions from multiple witnesses—the man with white hair who visited Harvey's apartment, who rode in the cab with him, who might have met with him at an attorney's office. "Has Gregg been seen since Harvey disappeared?"

"Not that my sources know. But if Gregg was sent to stop Harvey from talking to police, and if Harvey has indeed disappeared, then Gregg has probably moved on to other assignments. Or he's lying low."

"What about the painting? The one from the Vesper estate?"

"That's the interesting part," Victor said, and for the first time there was something like satisfaction in his voice. "The painting was recovered. Three days ago, it was found leaning against the steps of the public library downtown. There was a note attached. It said, 'Property of the Vesper Estate. Return to family of Carl Vesper with apologies.' No signature. But the message was clear: someone wanted to return the painting but couldn't do it openly."

"That has to be Harvey."

"Almost certainly. Which means either he found a way to contact someone in the Kaslan operation and negotiate his exit, or he managed to get free of them and return the painting as a kind of final statement. Either way, he's showing courage. But courage doesn't keep you alive in this world."

"Is there anything else I should know?"

Victor stood, signaling that the interview was approaching its end. "There's a woman. I don't know her name, but she's connected to the operation somehow. Red hair, very beautiful, very cold. She came into the network about two years ago, and she's been Kaslan's personal acquisition specialist since then. Meaning she's the one who identifies valuable items and figures out how to extract them from current possession. She's also the one who eliminates complications."

"Complications like witnesses?"

"Complications like anyone who might talk. I only bring her up because my sources indicate she was seen meeting with Harvey about two weeks ago. And two weeks after a meeting with her, people tend to either disappear or die. The fact that Harvey disappeared suggests he may have negotiated with her. Or made a deal with her that required his complete withdrawal from the city."

"Do you have a name? A description?"

"The name she uses is Margot. That may or may not be real. The description I already gave you—red hair, beautiful, cold. But here's what matters: if she met with Harvey, it wasn't to kill him. It was to facilitate something. And that something was probably his exit from the Kaslan operation."

"What would that cost?"

"For Margot? Usually everything you have, and then your silence for the rest of your life." Victor moved toward the exit. "Your boy Harvey is either dead, or he's bought his freedom at the cost of his entire life. Either way, he's gone from this city. And Kaslan, despite losing the painting and losing Harvey, is still operating. Still acquiring. Still distributing. The Vesper painting was a loss—a significant one—but it's not enough to bring down the operation."

"What would bring it down?"

"Testimony," Victor said. "A full account of the network's operations, the names of the operatives, the locations of the storage facilities, the routes used for distribution. That would bring it down. And that's what Harvey had, or what Harvey could have provided. Which is almost certainly why, if he's still alive, he's living under a different name in a different city, terrified that one day Kaslan or Margot or Silas Gregg will find him and remind him why it's dangerous to develop a conscience."

---

**Detective Halloway's Assessment:**

Victor's account provides valuable intelligence on the Kaslan operation but raises critical questions regarding Harvey's current status. If the informant is accurate, subject possessed information that made him simultaneously valuable for law enforcement prosecution and extremely dangerous. His decision to consult Marchetti suggests intent to cooperate, but his disappearance suggests either:

1. He was apprehended and killed by Kaslan operatives (Silas Gregg the likely executor)
2. He negotiated safe passage through an intermediary (Margot the suspected negotiator)
3. He obtained independent means of escape (timeline suggests this less likely)

The recovery of the painting on library steps suggests either Harvey's attempt to make amends or a message to law enforcement from another party. Recommend:
- Identify "Margot" through photograph distribution to underworld sources
- Locate Silas Gregg through interstate law enforcement databases
- Contact Carl Vesper widow regarding painting's recovery
- Monitor Kaslan operation for signs of operational disruption or retaliation
- Place Harvey on all wanted persons lists with notation that he may be voluntary missing person cooperating with investigation
