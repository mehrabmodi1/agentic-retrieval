# Witness Statement: Dorothy Helen Marchetti
## Executive Secretary, Marchetti & Associates Law Offices

**Date:** November 15, 1947  
**Time:** 10:00 AM  
**Location:** Marchetti & Associates, Suite 1450, Consolidated Building, Downtown  
**Interviewer:** Detective Frank Morello  
**Status:** Voluntary Statement

---

Dorothy Marchetti had been a secretary for seventeen years, and she possessed the particular skill set of someone who could read the invisible currents running through an office with the precision of a seismograph detecting earthquakes. She was thirty-four, wore her dark hair in waves that took an hour at the beauty parlor every two weeks, and had the kind of competence that made partners in law firms despise losing her to marriage. She had married once, briefly, decided the arrangement was detrimental to her professional growth, and returned to her maiden name and her desk with the kind of finality that precluded further discussion on the subject.

Her office was a model of organization: files aligned at perfect right angles, pens in a brass holder, calendar marked meticulously with appointments and deadlines. Detective Morello sat in the chair across from her, and she offered him coffee, which he accepted though she suspected he would not drink it.

"James Harvey came to this office three times," Dorothy said, consulting the appointment book though she didn't need to. She could have recited the dates from memory. "The first visit was September 8th. He met with Mr. Marchetti, my brother-in-law, for approximately two hours. The second visit was October 2nd, a much shorter meeting, perhaps thirty minutes. The third visit was October 28th, and that meeting lasted less than five minutes."

"What was the nature of these meetings?"

Dorothy hesitated slightly, and this hesitation was important. It indicated that she was wrestling with professional confidentiality against civic obligation. "I cannot discuss the details of the meetings. Attorney-client privilege. But I can tell you that Mr. Harvey's demeanor deteriorated significantly between the first and final visits."

"In what way?"

"During the first meeting, he was nervous but composed. He wore a suit, new-looking, and he carried a briefcase. He seemed like someone who was engaged in ordinary legal matters—perhaps incorporation, or estate planning, or standard contract work. Mr. Marchetti asked me to bring coffee, which he rarely does unless the client seems to require comfort. Mr. Harvey drank two cups and left his hands on the desk the entire time—visible, unthreatening."

Dorothy adjusted her glasses. There was something about her systematic description that suggested someone documenting forensic evidence. "The second visit was different. Mr. Harvey arrived without an appointment—he came to the office at lunch and asked if Mr. Marchetti could see him. He waited forty minutes, and during that wait, he did not sit still. He paced the reception area. He checked the elevator repeatedly. He seemed to be waiting for something else to occur, as though the appointment itself was a secondary concern."

"And the final visit?"

"The final visit was disturbing," Dorothy said quietly. She looked away from the detective, toward the window where the city stretched out beneath them like a mechanism that had broken down but continued operating anyway. "Mr. Harvey arrived at four o'clock in the afternoon. He looked worse. Much worse. He hadn't shaved, his collar was wrinkled, and he had that particular look that people have when they haven't slept in several days. When I told him Mr. Marchetti was with another client, he became almost frantic. He wanted to know exactly when Mr. Marchetti would be available. He asked if he could wait. He sat in that chair"—Dorothy pointed to the chair Morello currently occupied—"and for thirty-seven minutes, he did not move. He did not read the magazines. He did not look out the window. He simply sat with his hands together as if he was praying, and he stared at the wall."

"What happened when Mr. Marchetti saw him?"

"It was brief. He went into my brother-in-law's office, and less than five minutes later, he emerged. His face was very pale. He walked to the elevator without looking at me, and I heard him weeping as he waited for it to arrive. Not loud weeping. Quiet weeping, the kind of sound a man makes when he is trying to suppress it but cannot quite manage."

"Did Mr. Harvey ever discuss what he was seeing Mr. Marchetti about?"

"No. But between the first and final visits, I did research. It is not uncommon for me to assist Mr. Marchetti by gathering preliminary information on clients. I took the liberty of checking if there were any court filings, any outstanding legal matters, anything that might have brought Mr. Harvey to an attorney. There was nothing. No criminal record, no civil actions, no bankruptcy or financial crisis that would have necessitated legal counsel. So whatever brought him to this office three times, it was not standard legal work."

"Did Mr. Marchetti mention anything to you?"

Dorothy shook her head slowly. "My brother-in-law is extremely close about his professional matters. I would not expect him to discuss the details. But I will tell you something I observed. After the final visit from Mr. Harvey, Mr. Marchetti seemed disturbed. Not angry. Not frustrated. Disturbed in the way that someone is disturbed when they have been asked to participate in something that violates their ethical code but which they feel compelled to do anyway."

"Did he explain this?"

"The following day, he came to my desk and said, 'Dorothy, if anyone ever asks you about a client named James Harvey, you say nothing. You confirm no appointments, you verify no meetings, you admit no knowledge of him whatsoever.' I asked him if there was a legal matter that required this discretion, and he said, 'No. There's something worse than a legal matter. There's a moral one.'"

Detective Morello set down his untouched coffee. "Did he say anything else?"

"He said, 'Some people come to us expecting us to help them break the law legally. Others come to us in the hope that we'll help them avoid the consequences of having already broken it. The worst are the ones who come to ask us to help them do something that will break them entirely, but they do it anyway because they're already broken and they don't see how it matters anymore.'"

"Which category did he think Mr. Harvey fell into?"

"I asked him that exact question," Dorothy said. "He looked at me for a long moment, and then he said, 'All of them. He's all three at once. And before he leaves, he'll need to contact his neighbor—the woman in 3G—because she's the one who'll know what comes next.' And then he went back into his office and closed the door, and I heard him pour himself a whiskey even though it was barely eleven in the morning."

"Has Mr. Marchetti mentioned Mr. Harvey again?"

"Not once. But his behavior has changed. He seems older. There are times when I bring him documents to review and I find him staring at nothing, his hands just sitting on the desk. My brother-in-law is a man of precision, Detective. He was a trial lawyer for twenty years before he went into private practice. He does not become distracted easily. But since Mr. Harvey's final visit, he has become very easily distracted indeed."

Morello stood, and Dorothy stood with him, professional courtesy. "Is Mr. Marchetti available to speak with me?"

"He is in trial this week, but he will return to the office tomorrow afternoon. Shall I give him your message?"

"Please do. And tell him this is important."

Dorothy walked the detective to the door. As he was leaving, she said, "Detective, whatever Mr. Harvey was involved in, it was something that destroyed Mr. Marchetti's confidence in the law. A man of principle, broken by a client. That suggests something very serious indeed."

**Supplementary Note - November 16, 1947**

Detective Morello interviewed Robert William Marchetti, Esq., regarding his meetings with James Harvey. Mr. Marchetti declined to discuss specifics citing attorney-client privilege but confirmed three meetings occurred as stated. When pressed regarding the content, Mr. Marchetti stated: "I cannot speak to the substance of our conversations. What I can tell you is that Mr. Harvey presented me with a choice—I could help him, which would require me to compromise my professional ethics, or I could refuse, which would require me to accept that his resulting actions would be my responsibility in a moral if not legal sense. I chose to help him. Whether that was the right choice, I will never know."

Further attempts to clarify resulted in Mr. Marchetti requesting that this office contact his attorney before any additional questions were posed. Investigation shelved pending legal determination of privilege boundaries.

**Additional Notes by Detective Morello:** Subject utilized legal counsel for unknown purposes. Timing suggests final visit may have been request for assistance with disappearance or evidentiary matters. Marchetti's moral distress indicates request was unethical or illegal. Privilege prevents direct investigation. Recommend: Monitor Marchetti's communications, determine if he maintains contact with Harvey post-disappearance, examine financial records for unusual payments that might indicate Harvey employment or compensation.
