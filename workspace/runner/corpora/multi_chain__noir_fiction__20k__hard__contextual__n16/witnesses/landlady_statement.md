# Witness Statement: Evangeline "Eva" Kosinski
## Landlady, Riverside Apartments

**Date:** November 15, 1947  
**Time:** 2:15 PM  
**Location:** Riverside Apartments, 1247 Harbor Road, Landlady's Office (Unit 1A)  
**Interviewer:** Detective Sarah Chen  
**Status:** Voluntary Statement

---

The office smelled of vinegar and onion, though no food was visible. Eva Kosinski sat in a chair too small for her considerable frame, her hair pinned back so tightly it seemed to stretch her face into a permanent expression of skepticism. She was sixty-three, dressed in a gray dress that had been fashionable when Roosevelt was still alive, her thick hands folded over a ledger as though protecting the secrets it contained. The walls around us were lined with rent receipts, all meticulously filed, and a series of photographs: a younger Eva with a man who had to be her late husband, children with stiff expressions, grandchildren whose faces suggested a future of disappointment.

"Mr. Harvey?" she said, her accent still thick after fifty-two years in America. "Yes, I know this boy. Three years he lives in 3F. Always quiet. Always pays rent exactly on time—never a day late, not once. I have maybe two hundred tenants in this building, Detective. You know how many pay on time always? Maybe ten. Maybe five. Mr. Harvey is one of the five."

Detective Chen opened a small notebook. She had the kind of face that made people talk—not pretty, but honest, and there was something in her dark eyes that suggested she'd learned the value of listening to men and women nobody else bothered to hear. "Tell me about his habits."

"Habits?" Eva considered the word as though it were written in a foreign language she was learning to read. "He keeps strange hours. I notice because the building manager, he makes noise in the morning, six o'clock, six-thirty, and usually the tenants, they complain. Not Mr. Harvey. He is awake already. I can hear him walking, back and forth, back and forth, like he is pacing. Thinking, maybe. Worrying, more likely. A man doesn't pace like that unless something is eating him from the inside."

"Did you ever speak with him about his concerns?"

"We speak three, maybe four times in three years. Once, he asked if I could change the lock on his door. The lock was working fine, but he said he wanted a new one anyway. I told him the rent did not cover locks. He paid extra. One hundred dollars for a lock that costs maybe thirty. You know what that tells you? A man who wants to keep things out, or keep things in. Either way, he is afraid."

Eva adjusted her glasses. They were connected by a chain of small beads, and the gesture made her look momentarily academic, like a professor of the human condition preparing a lecture nobody wanted to attend. "The money, Detective. It is important you understand about the money. Three years, he gives me a check, and the check, it is always exactly the same amount. Three hundred seventy-five dollars for the apartment. Not a dime more, not a dime less. But six weeks ago, maybe seven, he comes to me and he says he will move. I ask why. He says the place is too noisy. But it is not noisy. This building, it is quiet. The walls are thick. The neighbors, mostly they are old people, or young families trying to be quiet so they don't disturb the old people. So I don't believe him. But I don't ask questions. I don't want him to think I am nosy."

"But you didn't let him leave," Chen observed.

"No," Eva said, and a slight smile crossed her face—not unkind, but the smile of a woman who had learned to play the long game, who understood that information was more valuable than rent. "I told him that the lease, it required two months' notice. Which was true. And I told him the apartment upstairs would be available in December, but not before. He seemed to relax when I said this. Not happy, you understand. But relieved. Like he had been given a reprieve from something."

"Did anyone visit him? Did you see anyone going to his apartment?"

"I see many people," Eva said. "This is a big building. But unusual visitors for Mr. Harvey?" She tapped her temple with one finger. "Once, I see a man who does not belong. Nice suit, good shoes, but the walk, you know? The walk of someone who does not walk much. Too stiff. Too deliberate. He goes up to three F—I see him in the hallway because the light, it is not very good on the stairs and I am always checking the lights. He stays maybe forty minutes. When he comes down, his face is different. Like he has given Mr. Harvey something very important, or taken something away."

"Do you have a description?"

"Fifties, maybe sixty. White hair, silver almost. Heavy in the middle. An accent, I think, but I did not hear him speak. But there is something about him that says he has traveled. The way he holds his body. The way he looks at things like he is cataloging them, like everything might be important later. A man does not move that way unless he has spent his life in dangerous places."

Chen wrote this down carefully. "When was this?"

"Three weeks ago. Maybe two weeks and five days. I remember because it was the day before I ordered new carpet for the downstairs hallway, and the man, he looked like he would notice the carpet. Like he would file away that we have worn burgundy carpet, so if he needed to report on the building to someone, he could say exactly what it looks like."

"Did Mr. Harvey seem different after this man's visit?"

Eva's hands tightened on her ledger. "He stopped pacing at night. Or maybe he paced quieter. And there was something in his eyes the next time I saw him—he came to pay rent, he always comes in person—something that said he had made a decision about something. A big decision. The kind that changes your life. And once, when I was checking the lights in the stairwell, I heard him on the telephone in his apartment. He was saying something about 'the other location'—he kept repeating it. 'Get the package to the other location before he finds out.' And then the name came: 'The Marina. Use the Marina.' He was frantic. Whoever was on the phone, they were his lifeline."

"When did you last see him?"

"October 29th. He brings the rent check, and he seems lighter, like a weight has been removed. We speak about the weather—it is turning cold—and he says he might spend Thanksgiving with family. Family! In three years, he has never mentioned family. I don't even know if he has family. But that day he mentions it like he is making a promise to himself. Then he leaves."

"And you haven't seen him since?"

"No. But I begin to worry when he does not come out, not for three days, not for four. So on the fourth day, I go up to the apartment. I have a master key—for emergencies, in case someone is sick, has died, has become a danger to themselves. I knock first, loud. No answer. I use the key. The apartment, it is empty. His clothes are gone. His furniture, most of it. He left behind only what could not be carried—the old sofa that was here when he moved in, the kitchenette, things like that. But his personal things, his papers, his life—it is all gone. Except one thing. A letter, left on the kitchen table, addressed to me. He had written: 'If anyone asks about my apartment or my whereabouts, tell them nothing. But if a detective comes asking about evidence or a location called the Starlight, tell him that I moved everything. That I realized the first place was too obvious. The Harbor Trust Bank downtown. Box 447. That's where the real truth is kept.'"

"Did he leave any forwarding address?"

"Nothing. He just... vanishes. Like he was never here. But I know he was here because I have the rent checks, and I have the memory of a man who was quiet and careful and afraid. Those things, you cannot erase."

Detective Chen closed her notebook. "Did you ever see him with a young woman? A redhead?"

Eva shook her head. "I would have noticed. In this building, we do not see many beautiful young women visiting one young man's apartment. It would have been unusual. No, Mr. Harvey, he lived alone. Completely alone. That much I am certain of."

"One more question," Chen said. "Do you think he left because he wanted to, or because he had to?"

Eva was quiet for a long moment. She looked out the window at the gray street below, where people moved like ghosts through the afternoon. "A man pays his rent on time for three years, and then he disappears. He never complains, never asks for anything, except to change a lock. He mentions family he has never spoken of before. And then he is gone." She turned back to Chen. "That is not a man who wanted to leave. That is a man who was running. From what, I do not know. But he was running very fast, and he was running from something very serious."
