# Witness Statement: Eleanor Ruth Pembroke
## Neighbor, Riverside Apartments, Unit 3G

**Date:** November 15, 1947  
**Time:** 3:45 PM  
**Location:** Riverside Apartments, Unit 3G, Living Room  
**Interviewer:** Detective Sarah Chen  
**Status:** Voluntary Statement

---

Eleanor Pembroke was seventy-two years old, a widow for thirteen years, and the kind of woman who maintained the kind of meticulous existence that comes from having little else to occupy her time except observation of the lives of others. She knitted, read mystery novels, and maintained a running commentary on the comings and goings of her neighbors that would have made a professional surveillance operative envious. Her apartment was small and immaculate, with doilies on the armrests and photographs of people who had all presumably disappointed her in some way but who deserved to be remembered anyway.

Detective Chen sat in the chair Eleanor offered, declining tea but accepting the fact that it would be forthcoming regardless.

"I know the boy next door," Eleanor said without preamble. "James. A nice boy. Quiet. The kind of quiet that suggests someone has learned that noise brings attention, and attention brings trouble." She began knitting as she spoke—a blue sweater that would take her weeks to complete and that nobody would ever wear. "I've lived in this building for twenty-three years. You learn about people. You notice patterns. The boy, his pattern was: come home, sit quietly, wake early, leave quietly. Repeat. Five days a week, this pattern. On weekends, he might go out once, perhaps to buy groceries or newspapers. But mostly he stayed in his apartment."

"Did you ever speak with him?"

"Once, in the hallway. Six months ago, perhaps longer. He was carrying a box, and he dropped it. I helped him pick up the contents. It was books. Literary novels, mostly. Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, James. The kind of reading that suggests a man is trying to understand something about himself that he cannot otherwise understand. I made a comment about the authors, and he asked if I'd read them. I said I had, a long time ago, when I was young enough to think literature could answer the questions that life asked. He said, 'Has it stopped being able to do that?' And I said, 'No. But now I'm smart enough to know which questions literature can answer and which ones it can't.'"

Eleanor's needles clicked rhythmically. The sound was soothing in the way that repetitive noise can be.

"That was our conversation. Perhaps three minutes total. But I noticed something about him when he talked about books—the tension in his face relaxed. Like he was discussing the one thing that still connected him to whatever he was before he became the person he was trying to be."

"Did you notice anyone visiting him?"

"One visitor. A woman. Three weeks ago, perhaps a bit less. October 15th or 16th, I believe. She was young, redheaded, beautiful in a way that made her seem almost unreal. She came to his door in the evening at about 8:45 PM, and I saw her from my peephole just out of curiosity. He opened the door, and his face transformed. It was the same transformation I'd seen when he talked about books, but more extreme. Like he had been electrified. She arrived carrying a small package wrapped in brown paper, and when she left, she had nothing with her."

"Did you see her leave?"

"She left perhaps an hour later. When she came out of his apartment, she was crying. Not sobbing. Just tears running down her face while she maintained complete composure. She wiped her eyes before she went to the elevator, but not before I saw them."

Chen wrote this down. "What did her visit seem to indicate?"

Eleanor paused in her knitting. "Goodbye. It seemed like goodbye. You reach a certain age, Detective, and you've seen enough couples in love to recognize the difference between 'hello' and 'goodbye.' The way they held hands for perhaps longer than necessary, the way their conversation seemed important but sad—these things suggested an ending."

"Do you think she was his lover?"

"No. I think she was more important than that. I think she was his conscience, or his redemption, or perhaps the person who represented the life he could have had if things had gone differently. I think she was the reason he was struggling so hard with whatever he was struggling with."

"After that visit, did you notice any changes in his behavior?"

"Yes. He stopped pacing. For the first months he lived here, I could hear him at night, walking back and forth in his apartment. This went on for hours sometimes. But after the woman visited, the pacing stopped. It was replaced by... stillness. The kind of stillness that comes when a decision has been made."

Eleanor set down her knitting. "Shall I tell you what I think happened, Detective? You may not care for the opinion of an old woman who has only met her neighbor once, but I'll tell you anyway."

"Please do."

"I think the boy was involved in something that he didn't want to be involved in. Something that made him unhappy and made him pace at night. I think he consulted with someone—a lawyer, presumably, based on the men I saw visiting the building—about how to extract himself. I think the woman was someone from his past, or someone from the situation he was trying to leave, and she came to say goodbye and to tell him that leaving was the right choice, even though it would be difficult and dangerous. I think he spent several days after her visit making arrangements for his departure. And then he left. Not because he wanted to, but because it was the only choice that allowed him to continue living with himself."

"That's quite a theory."

"It is not a theory," Eleanor said, picking up her knitting again. "It is an observation of the human condition from someone who has spent the last several decades watching people struggle with their moral failures. Your boy next door was a man trying to be better than he had been. Whether he succeeded or not, I don't know. But I know that he tried."

"Did you ever see anyone else visiting him? Any men, particularly?"

"One. Heavy-set, white-haired man. He came after the woman. Once, maybe twice. He and the boy did not speak much when I saw them in the hallway. But there was a quality of respect between them. The kind of respect that comes from understanding something about each other that doesn't require words."

"What do you think this man wanted?"

"I think he was giving the boy options. I think he was explaining what would happen if the boy tried to leave, and what would happen if the boy stayed. I think he was representing an organization or person who wanted to maintain control over the boy. And I think the boy listened to what he had to say and made a decision anyway."

Eleanor's needles continued their rhythmic clicking. "You see, Detective, there is a point in a person's life when they understand that they will die someday. Not intellectually—people understand that at a much younger age. But viscerally. They understand that the time they have left is limited and valuable and that the way they spend it matters. The boy seemed to reach that point. He seemed to understand that whatever the consequences were of his actions, they would be better than the consequences of his inaction."

"Do you think he's still alive?"

Eleanor didn't answer immediately. She finished a row of knitting, set down the needles, and looked out the window at the city beyond. "I think that if he is alive, it is because the people he was trying to escape decided that letting him go was better than trying to keep him. And I think that if he is dead, then the people around him—particularly the redheaded woman—failed to protect him when it mattered most. But what I think most likely is that he made an arrangement with someone, and that someone helped him leave, and that he is alive somewhere else now, trying to figure out how to be a better person than he was, trying to understand those literary questions that he couldn't answer before."

"Would you recognize the white-haired man if you saw him again?"

"I would. But I hope you don't ask me to identify him. I don't think he was a bad man, exactly. I think he was a man who worked for bad people, and who had to do bad things, and who was trying to do good things within the constraints of his situation. When you reach my age, Detective, you understand that most people are like that. We're all trying to do good things within the constraints of situations we didn't choose."

Chen closed her notebook. "Thank you, Mrs. Pembroke."

"Before you go," Eleanor said, "I want to tell you one more thing. The last time I saw the boy, it was the night before he disappeared. I was taking out my trash, and he was leaving his apartment. He was carrying that small suitcase, and his face was determined—but not desperate. There was a quality of intention in his movement, as though every action was deliberate, purposeful. He packed that suitcase not like someone fleeing in panic, but like someone executing a carefully considered plan. I said good luck to him, though I didn't expect him to hear me. But he did. He turned and looked at me, and he said, 'For the first time in a long time, Mrs. Pembroke, I think I'm going to be okay.' And then he left. Whether that was true or not, I cannot say. But I believed him when he said it."

Eleanor picked up her knitting again. "Find him, if you can. And if you do, tell him that Mrs. Pembroke in 3G thinks he made the right choice."

**Detective Chen's Addendum:**

Subject appears to have maintained awareness of the moral consequences of his actions throughout his involvement in the Kaslan operation. Neighbor's testimony suggests subject experienced internal conflict manifesting as nighttime pacing and sleep disturbance. The redheaded woman's visit appears to have been precipitating event for subject's decision to leave—whether through mutual decision or ultimatum remains unclear.

White-haired man (believed to be Silas Gregg) may have presented subject with limited options: full separation with assistance, or violent consequences. Subject's stated confidence that "he would be okay" suggests he believed he had secured safe passage.

Neighbor demonstrates exceptional observational skills. Her assessment that subject attempted moral redemption is consistent with legal consultation, painting recovery, and cooperative stance toward law enforcement. She should be considered credible source for identifying Gregg if confrontation becomes necessary, though her reluctance to assist with formal identification suggests possible sympathy for his situation.

Final assessment: Subject succeeded in exit, believed he had secured protection, and departed with determination rather than desperation. Alive or dead, subject appears to have achieved internal peace regarding his situation before disappearance.
