# Chapter 1: The Phone Call at Three in the Morning

The rain hammered the office window like it had a personal vendetta. I was three fingers deep into a bourbon that had seen better years, watching the neon signs below bleed their colors into the wet street. The city smelled like it always did after dark—wet concrete, someone else's cigarettes, and the faint perfume of desperation that clung to every corner of this town like morning fog that never lifted.

My name is Jack Shepherd, and I've been a private investigator long enough to know that nothing good ever comes after midnight. That's when I saw her name on my office window, backwards in the rain-streaked glass: SHEPHERD INVESTIGATIONS. The sign was peeling at the edges, just like everything else in my life.

The phone rang at three in the morning.

I let it ring twice before I picked up. In this business, you learn not to be too eager. Eagerness gets you killed, or worse—gets you involved in something that'll make you wish you were dead.

"Shepherd." My voice came out rough, the bourbon coating my throat like old paint.

"Mr. Shepherd?" The voice was male, older, scared. I'd heard plenty of scared voices in my time. This one had money in it—the scared of a man who was used to getting what he wanted and couldn't quite believe the world wasn't cooperating. "My name is Richard Halstead. I need your help. My daughter... she's missing."

I didn't say anything right away. Instead, I watched the rain and the city lights and thought about all the reasons this was going to end badly. Missing daughters brought cops, and cops brought trouble, and trouble was the one thing I'd spent the last five years trying to avoid.

"Where did you get my number?"

"A colleague recommended you. Said you were discreet. Trustworthy." The word stuck in his mouth like he wasn't used to asking for trust from anyone. "Said you don't ask a lot of questions about clients' backgrounds."

That would be Frank Delgado, a two-bit attorney who owed me a favor from the Carmichael case. I made a mental note to have a conversation with Frank about discretion and the consequences of throwing my name around like a hall pass.

"How long has she been missing?"

"Two days. Since Tuesday night." I could hear the tension in his breathing, the way he was gripping whatever he was holding. "I... I haven't called the police yet."

That was interesting. Rich guys with missing daughters usually started with the cops. The fact that Halstead was calling a private investigator in the middle of the night suggested one of two things: either he had a reason not to want police attention, or he'd received some kind of warning against involving them. Neither option improved my mood.

"Tell me about her."

"Her name is Catherine. Catherine Halstead. She's twenty-three years old. Beautiful girl—blonde hair, green eyes. She has a small crescent-shaped birthmark just below her left collarbone—barely visible unless you know to look for it. She was at a restaurant Tuesday evening with some friends. The Park House, over on Fifth. When they came out at around eleven, she... she separated from the group. Said she was going to walk home, that she needed air. They tried to stop her, but Catherine was stubborn. Always has been." His voice cracked a little. "She never came home."

I stood up and walked to the window, pressing my forehead against the cool glass. Below, the street was empty except for the occasional taxi prowling for late-night fares. The rain had turned the pavement into a dark mirror, reflecting the city's neon insides back at itself.

The business model became clear as Halstead talked. They didn't just grab girls randomly. They researched, they selected, they learned the family's financial capacity. The note in the package had mentioned a "negotiation period"—which meant the ransoms were calibrated to what each family could actually pay without triggering police involvement at the federal level.

"Have you received any contact? Any letters, phone calls, demands?"

There was a long pause. Long enough that I knew the answer before he said it.

"A package arrived this morning. No postage, no return address. Just left on the front step." Another pause. "It contained a photograph. It was Catherine, but... she was frightened. There was a note."

"What did it say?"

"It said: 'Don't call the police. We'll be in touch. Act normally. Act like nothing's happened.'"

I closed my eyes. This was exactly what I'd been afraid of. Not a simple missing person—something darker, something deliberate. This was a kidnapping, and kidnappings were like quicksand: the more you thrashed around, the deeper you sank.

"I'll need to meet with you. In person. Before the sun comes up—and that's non-negotiable. I don't know what you're mixed up in, Mr. Halstead, but right now, daylight is a luxury we can't afford."

"Anywhere you say."

"There's a diner called Rosie's, over on Eighth and Whitmore. Be there at five o'clock. Come alone. Don't tell anyone—and I mean anyone—that you're meeting me. Do you understand?"

"I understand. And Mr. Shepherd? Find my daughter. I don't care what it costs."

He hung up before I could tell him that money had nothing to do with whether I'd find his daughter, or whether finding her would mean bringing her back alive. In this city, the price of things and the ability to pay were two very different animals.

I looked at the bottle of bourbon on my desk. It was half-empty—or half-full, depending on whether you were an optimist or just hadn't learned your lesson yet. I poured the rest of it down the sink and made coffee instead. It was going to be a long night, and I needed to be sharp.

The city outside was soaked in darkness, the kind that comes right before dawn when the night has exhausted itself and morning hasn't bothered to show up yet. Somewhere in that darkness, a girl named Catherine Halstead was either scared or dead or something worse than both. And I had, against my better judgment, just agreed to find her.

I pulled out my notebook and wrote: *Catherine Halstead, 23, blonde, green eyes. Missing Tuesday 11 PM. Photo package Thursday morning. Kidnapping—demands likely coming. Meet Richard Halstead, Rosie's Diner, 5 AM.*

Outside, the rain kept falling. The city kept spinning. And somewhere between the two, my life had just gotten a lot more complicated.

I went back to the bourbon after all.
