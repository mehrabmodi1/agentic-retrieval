# Chapter 5: The Call

I was back in my office by noon, sitting at my desk with a pot of coffee that I'd made myself and a bottle of bourbon that I was pretending not to notice. The window behind me looked out onto the city in daylight, which always made it look better than it was. Sunlight forgave the city's crimes in a way that rain and darkness never did.

My phone rang at 2:17 PM. The time stuck in my mind because I was staring at my watch, waiting for the call that would either move the case forward or blow everything apart.

"Shepherd." I picked up on the first ring.

"Mr. Shepherd, this is Richard Halstead." His voice was different—tighter, more controlled, but with an undercurrent of panic that no amount of composure could hide. "They've called. They want... they want me to meet with someone tonight. To discuss terms."

"Are you calling from a safe location?"

"Yes. My office. I locked the door."

"Tell me everything. Word for word if you can."

He breathed deeply. "The call came in at two o'clock, on my private line. A man's voice, I didn't recognize it. He said, 'Catherine is alive and will remain alive as long as you cooperate.' Then he said, 'You're going to arrange a meeting with someone who works for you. Someone with access to your accounts. Someone you trust.' I asked who, and he said it didn't matter. That he wanted someone who could move money quickly and quietly."

My instinct was screaming. Money. It always came back to money. "How much?"

"He didn't say yet. He just said that someone would be in touch with more information within the hour. That the next contact would be with specific instructions. And Mr. Shepherd, he said—" Halstead's voice dropped. "He said that if I involved the police, if I involved anyone except the person I chose to help me, Catherine would... would be hurt in ways that would make death look merciful."

That was the kind of threat that didn't come from amateurs. That was the kind of threat that meant someone knew what they were doing and had done it before.

"You need to call Lieutenant Webb at the police department. Tell him everything."

"No." Halstead's refusal was absolute. "If I do that, they'll know. They're watching me, Mr. Shepherd. I can feel it. They'll know if the police get involved."

"With all respect, sir, you can't feel them. You just think you can. The mind does that when it's afraid. But kidnappers don't announce themselves. They work in silence."

"Will you help me or not?"

The question hung in the air between us. Webb had warned me off. The police were officially involved. The right play was to step back, let the professionals handle it, hope that everything worked out okay. The professional play. The safe play.

"Tell me who works for you. Who has access to your accounts."

Halstead started listing names—a comptroller named Morris, an associate named Delgado (a different one from my attorney friend, though I made a note to check), a senior manager named Collins. Men who'd spent years earning Halstead's trust, which meant whoever this was, they'd chosen perfectly. Whoever called knew details about Halstead's business, his vulnerabilities, his structure.

"Has anyone unusual been sniffing around your company recently? Anyone asking questions about your operations, your finances, your staff?"

"There have been... inquiries. From another company. They've been trying to buy me out for the last three months. Offering ridiculous sums of money. When I refused, the offers got better. More aggressive."

"What company?"

"Meridian Holdings. A consortium out of the eastern seaboard. I don't know much about them. They keep their operations quiet."

That was interesting. A company willing to pay extraordinary amounts of money to acquire another company. A company that apparently knew enough about Halstead's operations to orchestrate a kidnapping. A company that was using the kidnapping to force Halstead's hand.

"Listen to me carefully. You're going to do exactly what they ask. You're going to arrange a transfer of funds with one of your people. You're going to make it look as normal as possible. And you're going to record every conversation you have about it. Every phone call, every meeting, every detail. Can you do that?"

"I can try."

"Don't try. Do it. And every time there's contact—phone call, letter, anything—you're calling me immediately after. Even if you think it's dangerous to call. Even if you think you're being watched. You call me. Do you understand?"

"Yes. Yes, I'll do it."

I hung up and immediately placed a call to Frank Delgado, the attorney who'd recommended me in the first place. He was in his office, which meant I didn't have to wait. I asked him to do some research on Meridian Holdings—what their business was, who owned them, what their interest in Halstead's company might be. Frank's answer came within hours: Meridian was a shell corporation created specifically to acquire blackmail documents from city officials, financial ledgers showing hush payments, and compromising information.

"That's going to take a few hours," Frank said. "And it's going to cost you."

"Put it on Halstead's tab."

"Halstead's already your client?"

"Halstead's daughter is missing. And I'm about to break about five laws trying to find her before the police department's bureaucracy gets her killed. So yes, you could say I'm invested."

Frank made a noise that was part laugh and part groan. "You're going to get yourself killed one of these days, Jack."

"Probably. But not today."

I hung up and sat back in my chair, staring at the bourbon bottle. The smart thing to do was drink it, go home, and let the police handle the case. The smart thing had kept me alive for forty-three years. But it hadn't kept me from becoming someone I didn't particularly like, and it hadn't stopped the city from getting worse, one crime at a time.

There was a knock on my office door. I wasn't expecting anyone, which meant I checked the .38 Special in my desk drawer before I said, "Come in."

The woman who entered was trouble from the moment the door opened. She was maybe thirty, with dark hair and intelligent eyes and the kind of beauty that came from confidence rather than genetics. She was dressed in a navy suit that probably cost more than my monthly rent, and she carried herself like someone used to getting what she wanted.

"Mr. Shepherd? My name is Diana Harrow. I work for Meridian Holdings."

I hadn't been expecting a personal visit. Kidnappers didn't usually announce themselves. "Please, sit down."

"I'll stand, thank you." She remained by the door, as if positioning herself for a quick exit. "I'm here to offer you a job. Meridian Holdings is willing to pay you fifty thousand dollars to locate a specific person and provide us with information regarding their whereabouts and activities."

Fifty thousand dollars. That was the kind of money that changed lives. That was also the kind of money that usually meant something was desperately wrong.

"Who's the person?"

"His name is Harrison Webb. He's a lieutenant with the city police department. He's been conducting investigations into our company's activities, and we believe he may be in possession of documents that we require. If you can locate these documents and provide them to us, the payment will be made in full."

The same Webb who'd walked into Catherine's apartment. The same Webb who'd warned me off the case. The same Webb who apparently wasn't just investigating a kidnapping—he was investigating Meridian Holdings.

"I'm going to have to decline."

Diana Harrow smiled, and it was the smile of someone holding all the cards. "I think you'll reconsider once you understand the consequences of refusing. You see, Mr. Shepherd, you're already involved. You've been hired by Richard Halstead to investigate his daughter's disappearance. You've already violated police instructions by continuing to look into the case after being told to stop. And Catherine Halstead's safety depends entirely on you stepping back and staying out of things that don't concern you."

"Is that a threat?"

"It's a fact. Catherine Halstead is alive because Meridian Holdings has decided she has value. She'll remain alive as long as certain conditions are met and certain people stay out of our way. But if you continue to investigate, if you help Lieutenant Webb, if you in any way interfere with our business, Catherine will become... worthless."

She delivered the threat like she was discussing weather, which somehow made it more credible.

"I want to talk to Catherine. Proof of life."

Harrow reached into her jacket and pulled out a phone—a small portable device, the kind that was supposed to be the future but looked like a prop from a science fiction film. She dialed, waited, and then handed the phone to me.

"Catherine?" My voice came out harder than I intended.

There was a moment of silence, and then a girl's voice, small and frightened. "Mr. Shepherd? This is Catherine Halstead. I'm... I'm alive. Please don't do anything that will change that."

The line went dead.

Diana Harrow took the phone back. "Catherine is fine. She'll remain fine as long as you stay out of things. Make your choice, Mr. Shepherd. The city or the girl. You can't save both."

She walked out of my office, leaving the choice hanging in the air like smoke.

I sat at my desk for a long moment, staring at nothing, understanding that I'd just been outmaneuvered. Meridian Holdings knew about the investigation. They knew about Webb. They knew about Catherine. And they'd calculated that the only way to stop me was to make me choose between the case and the victim.

The problem was, I'd never been good at making the right choices. And I had a feeling that before this was over, Catherine Halstead would discover that loving the wrong person and taking the wrong walk had consequences that reached much farther than Fifth Avenue.

I picked up the phone and called Lieutenant Webb. His voicemail came on, so I left a message: "It's Shepherd. We need to talk. And you need to know that your investigation just became a kidnapping. Call me."

Then I turned back to my bourbon, poured a drink, and started thinking about a city where nothing was as it seemed and every choice led to blood.
