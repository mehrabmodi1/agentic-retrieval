# Chapter 3: The Park House

The Park House was the kind of restaurant that tried to look like it belonged in Paris, with cream-colored walls, art deco fixtures, and waiters who treated you like you were either very important or very poor—there was no middle ground. Tuesday nights were popular with the uptown crowd: lawyers, doctors, business people who wanted to see and be seen. The kind of place where a girl like Catherine Halstead would feel safe, surrounded by respectable people doing respectable things.

Which made it the perfect place to disappear.

I arrived at 6:55 AM and found the manager, a thin man named Colbert with slicked-back hair and the demeanor of someone who was about to have his establishment dragged into something unpleasant. He was expecting me because Halstead had called ahead. Money opened doors—and sometimes it opened mouths too.

The three friends were already waiting in a private room off the main dining area. Colbert showed me in with the expression of a man who wanted to be anywhere else.

The women were different enough that they probably complemented each other well. The first was dark-haired, serious-looking, with intelligent eyes and a habit of sitting very straight—Susan Marks, according to Halstead's notes, a lawyer. The second was more delicate, with reddish hair pinned carefully and hands that were never quite still—Eleanor Walsh, a secretary at some downtown firm. The third was heavier-set, with a broad face and an expression of genuine concern—Margaret Chen, who worked as a nurse.

"Thank you for meeting with me at this hour," I said, settling into a chair that was too comfortable for a serious conversation. "I'm investigating Catherine Halstead's disappearance. I understand you were with her on Tuesday evening."

"We were," Susan Marks said. She was the kind of woman who'd been taught early that confidence was armor. "We come to the Park House once a month. It's a tradition. Catherine, Eleanor, Margaret, and myself. We've been doing it for almost two years."

"Walk me through Tuesday night. Start from when you arrived."

Eleanor Walsh spoke up, her voice smaller but precise. "We arrived at seven o'clock. We had reservations for four. We usually have dinner and drinks for about three hours, sometimes longer if the conversation is good. Tuesday's conversation was... it was good. Better than usual, actually."

"Why's that?"

Margaret Chen exchanged a glance with the others. "Catherine had been preoccupied for the last few weeks. Distant. But Tuesday she seemed happier. More like herself. She laughed more. Made jokes. It was nice to see."

That was interesting. A girl who's about to be kidnapped doesn't usually show a marked improvement in mood. "Did she say what had been bothering her? Or what had changed?"

"She didn't want to talk about it," Susan said carefully. "And we respected that. Catherine has always been private about certain things. We've learned not to push."

"What certain things?"

The three women looked at each other again. There was some kind of communication happening between them, a silent negotiation about what could be said.

"Catherine was dating someone," Eleanor said finally. "She's been seeing him for a few months. She was very secretive about it, wouldn't tell us his name or much about him. But she was happy about it. We assumed it was going somewhere serious."

That was the kind of detail that changed everything. A secret boyfriend was either completely innocent or the key to the entire puzzle. "Do any of you know who this man is?"

"No," Susan said. "Catherine was adamant about keeping it private. We knew only that he was older, successful, and apparently quite taken with her. She had a look about her—the look of someone who's being taken care of, if you know what I mean."

I knew exactly what she meant. The look of someone who was being kept. "Describe the restaurant and the street when you left. I want to know the layout, the lighting, how crowded it was."

Eleanor pulled out a small notebook—she'd come prepared. "We left at approximately 10:45 PM. The restaurant was quiet by then, most of the crowd had cleared out. When we went outside, there were a few couples walking, a couple of cabs, but it was relatively calm. The street lights were on. It wasn't particularly dark."

"And Catherine decided to walk home."

"Yes," Margaret said. Her voice had a note of guilt in it. "We tried to convince her otherwise. The neighborhood is safe, but it was late, and none of us felt comfortable with her being alone. We were going to share a cab, and we offered to drop her first or take her the whole way."

"What was her exact reason for walking?"

"She said she needed air. She said the evening had been wonderful but she needed some time alone to think. She said..." Eleanor paused, remembering. "She said she had some decisions to make, and she thought better when she was walking."

That was a detail worth considering. Decisions suggested change. Change suggested complications.

"Which direction was she walking?"

"North, toward her apartment. She lives—lived, you understand—in the residential district, about ten blocks away. It's a pleasant walk. Safe."

"Had she done this before? Left alone at night from the restaurant?"

"No," Susan said firmly. "That was unusual. We always made sure she had safe transportation."

I stood up and walked to the window. The restaurant overlooked a small park, manicured and genteel in the morning light. Somewhere near here, Catherine Halstead had stepped out into the night and been taken. No sign of struggle, no evidence of violence. She'd simply disappeared.

"I'm going to need your phone numbers and addresses. And I'm going to ask you not to discuss this with anyone. Not friends, not family, not each other unless absolutely necessary. The men who took Catherine left a note asking that police not be involved, and they meant it. If word gets out that someone is investigating, it could complicate things. Do you understand?"

All three nodded. They understood. Fear was a good teacher.

I turned back to them. "One more thing: has Catherine ever mentioned the name of her father's business associate? Anyone she seemed nervous about, or uncomfortable around?"

The three women looked blank.

"Or has Catherine ever expressed concern about her father's work? Worry about the company, about money, about threats?"

Still nothing. They were looking at me like they were searching for a answer they simply didn't have.

I handed them each a card with my office number. "If you remember anything else—anything at all that seemed odd or significant—you call me immediately."

As I was leaving, Eleanor Walsh caught my arm gently. "You'll find her, won't you? You'll bring her back?"

I didn't have the heart to tell her that finding someone and bringing them back were two entirely different things. I simply said, "I'm going to do everything I can," which was true enough and meant almost nothing.

The morning was fully light when I stepped out onto the street. The Park House was on Fifth Avenue, in the heart of the good part of the city where nothing bad was supposed to happen. And yet, it had. A young woman with a secret boyfriend and decisions to make had walked out into the night, and the city had swallowed her whole.

I walked north, retracing the route Catherine would have taken. The neighborhood was exactly what Eleanor had described: well-lit, quiet, respectable. The kind of place where you wouldn't expect violence or theft or kidnapping. The kind of place where people trusted that safety was a given.

As I traced the northern path toward her apartment, I realized something that made my skin cold: the route passed directly adjacent to the harbor district. The warehouses. The importing operations. The places where money moved in and out without customs declarations. Catherine's walk home wasn't random. It was a route that went past the exact locations where the city's hidden economy operated. Someone had known she would walk this way. Someone had been waiting.

That was the city's most dangerous lie. Safety was never given. It was only ever rented, and the rent was always due.
