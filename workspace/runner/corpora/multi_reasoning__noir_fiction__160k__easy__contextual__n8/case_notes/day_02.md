# Day Two: The Unraveling

**Date:** November 5, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 8:22 AM - 2:15 AM (next day)

---

## Morning Moves

Wake up at six-thirty with that feeling you get when your brain has been working on a problem while you were sleeping. The kind of clarity that only comes in the dark hours. By eight I'm at the Police Records office on Third, talking to my contact, a clerk named Dorothy who trades information for lunch money and the occasional case of good whiskey.

"Claire Hollister," I tell her, spelling it. "Age twenty-three, resident of 447 Sycamore Drive."

Dorothy doesn't find much. A traffic citation from March, parking ticket, nothing serious. The girl didn't have a criminal record. Didn't even have the kind of record that suggests being in the wrong place at the wrong time. Clean.

But here's where it gets interesting: There's a notation in the system. "Cross-reference to Margaret Hollister real estate holdings." Someone—and I'm guessing that someone is Sergeant Krantz—already made the connection I was working toward.

"What does that mean?" I ask Dorothy.

She looks uncomfortable. "Means someone flagged it for follow-up. Means there might be something in the background worth checking." She hands me the reference number. "That's all I can tell you without getting trouble."

I buy her lunch anyway—pastrami sandwich at Feldman's down the street. She deserves it for being honest about the limits of her knowledge.

## Margaret Hollister's Business

By ten a.m. I'm at Hollister Realty on Fifth Avenue, fourth floor above a bookstore that's been going out of business for about five years. Margaret's office is small but expensive—leather chairs, a mahogany desk that probably cost more than my car, windows with a view of the city that most people pay for.

Margaret looks worse than yesterday. Hasn't slept. Probably hasn't changed clothes either, just different lipstick to cover up the falling apart underneath.

"You've found something," she says. It's not a question.

I tell her about the cross-reference in the police files. I tell her that Krantz is suspicious of something. I don't tell her what, because I don't know.

Margaret's face goes white. Not the white of shock. The white of confirmation. She knew something was going to come out. She's been waiting for it.

"My daughter was not... involved with anything illegal," Margaret says carefully, choosing her words like she's walking through a minefield. "If there are records, they're there for a reason. But not because of Claire."

I wait. In my experience, if you wait long enough, people fill the silence with truth.

"My business," Margaret continues, "involves purchasing properties. Older properties, sometimes. Neglected buildings. I rehabilitate them and sell them for profit. It's a good business. But in this city, a good business sometimes requires... cooperation."

There it is.

"What kind of cooperation?" I ask.

"The kind that involves making sure certain people are satisfied. Certain city officials. Certain business interests who prefer not to have their names connected to transactions."

Margaret stands up and goes to the window, looking out at the city like it's betraying her. "I'm not a bad woman, Mr. Halloran. But I'm not naive either. If I want to build a successful business in this city, I need to understand how the city works. And the city doesn't work the way people pretend it does."

"Who are these business interests?" I ask.

Margaret turns back to me. "People who could hurt my daughter. Which is why when she disappeared, I didn't go to the police. I came to you."

That changes things. That changes a lot of things.

## The Envelope

Margaret hands me a photograph. It's Claire, but not the secretary version. This Claire is dressed fancy, in a nightclub, sitting next to a man I recognize from the society pages. His name is Vincent Castellano, and he's the nephew of a man who supposedly handles "imports and exports" in the harbor district.

The Castellano family isn't the biggest criminal organization in the city, but they're significant. The kind of significant that makes cops nervous and judges more nervous.

"I don't know how Claire met Vincent," Margaret says quietly. "I only know it was wrong. I told her to stop seeing him. I told her there would be consequences. And then she disappeared."

"You think Castellano took her?"

"I think someone took her. Whether it's Vincent or someone else in that family, I don't know. What I know is that Claire got involved with people she shouldn't have gotten involved with, and now she's gone."

## The Harbor

By noon I'm down at the waterfront, where the city's money flows in and out of boats registered in countries that don't exist on American maps. The smell of salt and diesel and something darker underneath. The kind of place where asking questions can get you answers or get you killed, sometimes both.

I find a guy named Paulie who works at one of the import houses. I buy him a drink at a bar called the Anchor that hasn't been updated since about 1925. Paulie's nervous—the kind of nervous that suggests he knows more than he wants to talk about.

"The Castellano kid was in here Tuesday night," Paulie tells me after the second drink. "Before he disappeared."

"He disappeared?"

"Him and some girl. Tuesday night they were here, Wednesday morning nobody's seen them. The family's been quiet about it, which means bad things. When Castellano goes quiet, someone's bleeding."

Paulie doesn't know the girl's name. But he describes her: young, brown hair, pretty, looked scared but trying not to show it. That's Claire.

"They left with a man," Paulie continues. "Older guy, dangerous looking. Not Castellano family. Someone else. Someone important."

"You get a name?"

Paulie shakes his head. "I'm still alive because I don't ask names. But I'll tell you something—Vincent Castellano is supposed to be dead."

That hits different. I order another drink.

"How dead?" I ask.

"The kind of dead where there's no body yet, but everyone knows it's coming. The kind of dead where you don't talk about it out loud because it's still unconfirmed but inevitable. His father, Carmine Castellano, went into the office Tuesday night looking normal. He came out Wednesday morning looking like a man who'd just lost something valuable."

I pay Paulie for the drinks and the information. He doesn't want the money, but I leave it anyway. In this business, people take the money because turning it down would mean they remember you. Taking it is easier on everyone.

## Detour: Cocaine and Corpses

Before I leave the waterfront, I make a stop at a precinct I know. Specifically, I find Detective Morris, who's on the Castellano family watch. Morris is about fifty, gray at the temples, the kind of cop who's seen enough crime to know that the law is mostly for show.

"What's your interest in the Castellanos?" Morris asks, which is his way of saying he won't tell me anything without knowing why I'm asking.

I tell him about Claire. I don't tell him about Margaret's business.

Morris leans back in his chair. "I can't help you with official channels," he says. "But I can tell you that Vincent Castellano disappeared Tuesday night, and nobody's seen him since. The family's claiming he's on a business trip to Florida. Nobody believes it."

"Is he dead?"

"Not yet. But he will be. That's how these things go. You fuck up bad enough, you turn into a ghost. Then you turn into a memory. Then you get forgotten."

"What'd he fuck up?"

Morris looks at me for a long time. "The girl," he finally says. "That's what he fucked up. He fell in love with someone he shouldn't have. Or he stole something that belonged to someone important. Or both. With the Castellanos, it's always a combination."

I ask Morris about Thomas Mercer. Morris says Mercer's a lawyer, handles some of Castellano's business, stays on the right side of legal and the wrong side of ethical. Nothing I didn't already guess.

"The man who picked them up," Morris says carefully, "if there was a man, would have been from a higher up. Not Castellano family. Higher."

"How much higher?"

"Let's just say the kind of high where you don't ask questions, and you're still too high to get answers."

## Second Meeting with Margaret

Back at her office by four p.m. Margaret's been crying, but she's pulled herself together. The lipstick is fresh, the clothes are changed, but her eyes are still those of a woman who knows her child is gone and that knowing doesn't make it any easier.

I tell her what I found. I don't tell her all of it—I leave out the parts about Vincent being marked for death, because I don't think she could handle that right now.

"Someone important," Margaret repeats. "Someone above the Castellano family."

"That's what I'm guessing."

"My God," Margaret says, and there's something in her voice like a door closing. "Do you understand what that means? It means there's nothing I can do. It means I spent all this money and it doesn't matter because Claire's already gone in a way that money can't fix."

I don't tell her I think she's right. Instead, I take her retainer and leave her with promises I'm not sure I can keep.

## Evening Theory Session

Back at my apartment, I lay out what I know:

1. Claire Hollister disappeared with Vincent Castellano on Tuesday night from the Regency Hotel.
2. They left with a third party—a man, older, dangerous, important.
3. Vincent Castellano is now functionally dead, whether his body is or not.
4. Margaret Hollister has connections to the kind of real estate deals that require paying off city officials.
5. Thomas Mercer represents the Castellano family and possibly knows more than he's saying.
6. The man at the Regency Hotel wore expensive cologne and was careful about evidence.

The thing that troubles me most: if Claire is gone with someone important enough to disappear the Castellano family's golden boy, then she's either extremely valuable or extremely dangerous. Or maybe both.

## The Visit

Around midnight, there's a knock on my apartment door. I don't get many visitors, and never anyone at midnight, so I open it carefully.

It's a man I've never seen before. Tall, well-dressed, the kind of physical presence that suggests he's used to getting his way. He doesn't have a gun visible, but I know he has one anyway.

"You've been asking questions about people who prefer privacy," the man says. His voice is cultured, educated, dangerous.

"I've been asking questions about a missing girl."

"Yes. And the girl is not a matter for a private investigator. The girl is resolved. You should consider the matter resolved as well."

I don't move. I don't show anything. "Who are you?"

"Someone who represents interests that prefer discretion. The Hollister girl is gone. That's not changing. Asking more questions is not going to bring her back. What it will do is make people unhappy. Do you understand what I'm telling you?"

"I understand you're telling me to drop the case."

"I'm telling you that the case is already finished. The sooner you accept that, the longer your life continues to be interesting."

The man leaves. Just turns around and walks down the stairs like he owns the building. I stand in the doorway, listening to his footsteps echo down the hallway.

Then I close the door and pour another drink.

The case just became a lot more complicated.

I make a final note before sleep: Margaret mentioned that the company pursuing her real estate holdings—Meridian Holdings—has been trying to acquire her properties for months. The timing is significant. The acquisitions started three months ago. The payments they've offered her are far above market value. The offers became "more aggressive" when she refused. Now Claire is missing, and Margaret is desperate enough to hire me. The missing girl is leverage. The leverage is designed to force the sale of properties that Meridian Holdings needs. The question is: why do they need specifically those properties? What's on Margaret's land that's valuable enough to engineer a kidnapping?

## Notes for Tomorrow

- Find out who Vincent Castellano actually is beyond newspaper mentions
- Trace the man who visited my apartment—someone in that organization knows him
- Look into whether the Castellano family is involved in the missing persons bureau or keeping it quiet
- Dig deeper on Thomas Mercer's clients and connections
- Check morgues, hospitals, and accident reports for the past 48 hours
- Consider whether Claire Hollister is actually missing or actually dead

Something about this case smells like a setup. The kind of setup where the client knows more than they're saying, and the city's machinery is already working against you. I've worked plenty of cases, but this is the first time I've had a midnight visitor telling me to stop.

That means I'm getting close to something.

That also means I'm getting into deeper water.

The question now is whether I keep swimming or whether I learn to drown.
