# Chapter 2: The Weight of Money

Rosie's Diner was the kind of place that had seen better decades. The chrome fixtures were pitted with time, the red vinyl booths had more duct tape than original material, and the coffee had the consistency of engine oil and the taste to match. But Rosie's had two things going for it: the waitress didn't ask questions, and the back booth was private enough that you could discuss the darker aspects of human nature without the whole city hearing about it by noon.

Richard Halstead was already there when I arrived at 4:55 AM, which told me something about his state of mind. A man worried about his daughter doesn't sleep. He doesn't eat. He sits in dark diners with his hands wrapped around a cold cup of coffee and thinks about all the ways he's failed her.

He was fifties, maybe sixty—hard to tell with men who'd spent their whole lives pushing money around. The kind of aging that money can't quite hide: the soft around the jawline, the permanent creases around the eyes, the exhaustion that came from never being satisfied. His suit was expensive, charcoal gray with a silk tie that probably cost more than my monthly rent. But money doesn't help when you're afraid. Fear strips everyone down to the same bare frame.

I slid into the booth across from him. Rosie came over without being asked and poured two cups of the terrible coffee. Some habits, even diner waitresses understood without discussion.

"You look like you haven't slept," I said.

"I haven't." Halstead's hands were shaking slightly as he lifted the cup to his lips. He drank it anyway, not tasting it. "I've been thinking about Catherine. About all the things I should have done differently. All the times I was too busy, too focused on business. I would give everything I have to have those moments back."

That was the part people didn't understand about kidnapping. It wasn't just about the victim—it was a mirror held up to every choice the family had made, every moment they'd taken for granted. It was guilt crystallized into terror.

"Tell me about Catherine. Not the official version—the real one."

Halstead stared into his coffee like it might contain answers. "Catherine is brilliant. Not just intelligent—brilliant. She graduated from university at twenty with advanced degrees in literature and philosophy, both honors. Wanted to be a writer, actually. But when she came back to the city, she started working for the cultural foundation downtown, helping organize exhibitions and museum programs. Modest salary, not what her education could have gotten her, but work that mattered to her."

"Does she have any enemies? Anyone who might want to hurt her?"

"No. Catherine is gentle, thoughtful. She volunteers at a women's shelter on weekends. She gives money to charities. She's one of the few good things in this city, Mr. Shepherd, and I—" His voice cracked. He steadied it with effort. "I'm terrified that her goodness is what's gotten her killed."

I let that hang there for a moment. There was wisdom in what he said. Good people in a bad city were always at risk. It was like being a lamb in a room full of wolves who hadn't eaten in weeks.

"Tell me about the package. I need details."

He pulled out a manila envelope and slid it across the table. Inside was a Polaroid photograph, still fresh enough that I could smell the chemicals. It showed a young woman—blonde hair, green eyes, the kind of face that suggested her father was right about at least one thing. She was sitting on a bare wooden chair in what looked like a basement or warehouse. Her hands were folded in her lap. Her eyes were wide with fear.

And behind her, barely visible in the shadows, was another figure. Just enough to know someone was there. Just enough to know they were watching her.

I handed the photo back before my face could show what I was thinking. "The letter?"

Halstead produced it—typed on plain paper, words cut from newsprint like something out of a pulp novel. It said exactly what he'd told me: *Don't call the police. We'll be in touch. Act normally. Act like nothing's happened.*

"How many people know that Catherine is missing?"

"Just her mother. And my business partner, though I didn't tell him directly—he called wondering why Catherine hadn't come to her mother's birthday dinner on Wednesday. I had to explain." Halstead leaned forward, and I could see the plea in his eyes. "You have to find her before they contact me again. You have to find her before they ask for something I can't give them. The men I've dealt with—they're not acting independently. There's a structure, a hierarchy. Someone above Sinclair, someone orchestrating these operations. Someone who makes decisions that people like Sinclair execute. That's the real power in this city."

That was the problem with money—it created the illusion that you could buy your way out of anything. But some prices couldn't be paid in dollars. Some debts were settled in blood. And Halstead's real estate development business, it turned out, was the perfect vehicle for Meridian Holdings to move laundered money—property transfers with inflated valuations, cash payments disguised as legitimate acquisitions.

""What's your business, Mr. Halstead?"

He hesitated, and I realized that question had more weight than he wanted to admit. "Real estate," he said. "Development, mostly. Large projects that require coordination with city officials, with planning boards, with people who have the authority to approve or deny projects worth millions of dollars."

In other words, his business required him to operate at the interface between legitimate money and the kind of money that didn't ask questions. And Catherine being taken meant someone wanted to ensure he remained cooperative, that he continued to funnel what needed to be funneled. The hierarchy above the Castellano family wasn't just a rumor—it was a system where men like Halstead, and lawyers like Mercer, and facilitators like Harvey, all played their assigned roles."

"Import-export. Primarily from South America and Europe. Textiles, some specialty goods." He said it carefully, which meant there was more to it. There always was.

"And these specialty goods—they wouldn't happen to include anything that might interest someone enough to kidnap your daughter?"

He looked away, out the window at the pre-dawn gray that was starting to seep into the darkness. "I have had... dealings. With people who aren't always on the right side of the law. Nothing violent, nothing involving drugs. But yes, there are people who wouldn't mind having leverage against me."

That made it worse, not better. It meant this might not be a simple ransom situation. It meant there could be politics involved, power plays, and in this city, those situations had a way of ending with bodies and no answers.

"I need to see where Catherine disappeared. The Park House. And I need to talk to the friends who were with her."

"I've arranged for them to meet you at the restaurant at seven. They'll cooperate however you need them to."

"Don't promise things for other people, Mr. Halstead. It complicates matters." I stood up. The pre-dawn light was getting stronger, and I needed to move while the city was still half-asleep. "Here's what you're going to do: you're going to go home. You're going to act normally. If your wife asks questions, you tell her Catherine is safe but you need to keep this quiet for now. You're going to go to work. You're going to attend meetings. You're going to be a man whose daughter isn't missing. Can you do that?"

"Yes, but—"

"And you're not going to contact me unless something new happens. No checking in, no asking for updates. When they call—and they will call—you're going to listen to what they want and you're not going to give them any reason to think you've involved anyone else. Do you understand?"

He nodded, but I could see the fear in his eyes. He understood, but he didn't like it. Nobody does. Nobody wants to sit still when their child is in danger.

I pulled out a piece of paper and wrote my private number—the one I don't give to most people. "This number will reach me anytime. When they contact you, call me immediately after. Word for word, everything they say. Understood?"

"Yes. Thank you, Mr. Shepherd. I—"

"Don't thank me yet," I said. "We're a long way from a happy ending."

As I walked out of the diner, I could feel him watching me, hoping I could be the miracle he needed. But I'd been in this business long enough to know that there were no miracles in a city like this. There were only different varieties of bad outcomes, and the only question was which one you could live with.

The sun was starting to come up, painting the streets in shades of gray and amber. The city was waking up, and somewhere in it, a girl named Catherine Halstead was still in the dark.

I had seven hours until I met her friends, and a lot of questions that needed answering before then.
