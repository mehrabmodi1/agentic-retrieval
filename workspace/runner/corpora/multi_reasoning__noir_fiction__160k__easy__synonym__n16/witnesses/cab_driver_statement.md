# Witness Statement: Jerome "Jerry" Salvatore
## Cab Driver, City Cab Company, License #4427

**Date:** November 14, 1947  
**Time:** 7:45 PM  
**Location:** City Cab Company Garage, 89 Industrial Avenue, Dispatch Office  
**Interviewer:** Detective Michael Rossi  
**Status:** Voluntary Statement

---

Jerry Salvatore had been driving a cab for twenty-two years, which meant he'd seen approximately fourteen thousand variations of human desperation squeezed into the back of a yellow vehicle no bigger than a coffin standing on end. His face was the color of old newsprint, his hands permanently curved from gripping a steering wheel, and his eyes had that particular glaze that came from staring through windshields at a city that never quite made sense.

He smoked constantly, lighting one cigarette from the butt of another, and the cab that waited outside—parked in the garage bay like a horse in a stable—smelled like a tobacco processing plant.

"Harvey," Jerry said, rolling the name around in his mouth as though tasting it. "Yeah, I remember the kid. Couple of pickups over the last few months. Not a regular—I know my regulars, the businessmen who need to get to the airport at five in the morning, the society dames going to charity functions, the guys who work late and need a ride home before the trains stop running. But the kid, he was occasional. Maybe three, four times."

Detective Rossi sat in the chair opposite Jerry's, which was still warm from whoever had been sitting there before. The dispatch office was all noise and chaos—men shouting addresses, phones ringing constantly, the crackle of radios reporting traffic conditions and potential fares. It was the kind of place where order existed only in the dispatcher's mind and nowhere else.

"Where did you pick him up?" Rossi asked.

"Mostly from the Riverside Apartments. One time from a building on Fifth—some commercial thing, offices maybe. Another time from The Crimson Door, late night, maybe one in the morning. That was unusual because most of the people who need cabs from that place, they're so drunk they can barely give me an address. But the kid, he was sharp. Sober. He knew exactly where he wanted to go, and he was quiet about it. No loud talking, no trying to impress anyone. Just sat in the back and looked out the window like he was saying goodbye to something."

"Where did he go?"

"Different places. One time, some building in the warehouse district—I remember because those streets are narrow and the buildings are tall and it always feels like you're driving through a canyon that's about to collapse. Another time, a hotel. The Magnifico, out on the east side. That one struck me because a kid like that—he looked young, maybe late twenties—he shouldn't be staying at the Magnifico. That's the kind of place where old men go with young women who aren't their wives, and where deals happen that don't get written down anywhere."

"Did he seem nervous during these rides?"

Jerry tapped ash into a can at his feet—a practice that had probably saved the cab's upholstery more than once. "He was scared. Not of me, you understand. Not of the cab or the traffic or the city. He was scared of something internal. You learn to read people when you spend a quarter of your life alone with them in a small metal box. You watch their breathing, you listen to the way they handle their body, you notice if their hands are shaking when they count out the fare money. This kid, his fear was like a passenger taking up the whole backseat."

"Did you ever drive him with anyone else?"

"Once. That's the pickup I'm going to tell you about because it's the one that's stuck with me, the one that made me understand something was very wrong with whatever was going on in this kid's life." Jerry set down his cigarette in an overflowing ashtray. "I got a call—this was about two weeks ago, maybe more—to pick up a passenger at the corner of Sixth and Mercury. When I get there, there's the kid, and there's an older man waiting with him. Heavy-set, silver hair, the kind of face that had been important once. They both get in, and the kid gives me an address in the residential district—Oak Hill neighborhood, you know the one, all the big houses with the lawns and the gates?"

"What happened during the ride?"

"Nothing happened. That's what made it so wrong. They sat there, the old man and the kid, and they didn't exchange a word. Not one word in a fifteen-minute drive. But there was communication happening anyway, the kind that doesn't need sounds. It was all in the air between them. Tension, you know? The kind of tension right before something breaks. I've driven people on their way to break up, to confess things, to take actions that were going to destroy their lives. This felt like that."

"Did they seem threatening to each other?"

"No. No, it wasn't adversarial. It was something else. It was like... like a father and son who had reached a point where there was nothing left to say because all the important things had already been said, and now they were just going through the motions of being together." Jerry picked his cigarette back up. "When we got to the address, a big house set back from the road, the old man got out first. The kid stayed in the cab. I thought he was going to ask me to wait, but he said, 'Just drive.' And his voice was different when he said it. Broken somehow. So I drove."

"Did you ever see this man again?"

"No. But I'll tell you something strange. Three days after that pickup, I'm driving down Fifth Avenue, and I see the old man coming out of a building. He flags me down. I think he's going to need a ride, but he just leans in and looks at me real careful, and he says, 'If anyone asks, you didn't pick up any passenger with a young man a few days ago. If anyone asks, you didn't drive to Oak Hill. If anyone asks, you didn't see me.' And he hands me a hundred-dollar bill. A hundred dollars. For forgetting something that didn't even cost him anything."

"What did you say?"

"I took the money and I said, 'Didn't see a thing, sir.' Because I've learned something in twenty-two years of driving this cab. The people with a hundred dollars to forget something are usually people you should actually forget about. Getting involved with them tends to make your life worse rather than better."

Detective Rossi shifted in his chair. "Do you have any idea who this man was?"

"Not a clue. But I remember something he said to the kid when they first got in the cab. Real quiet, so quiet I almost didn't hear it. He said, 'It's done, then.' And the kid, he said back, 'It's done.' But whatever it was, it wasn't done. Something was still happening. Something was still going on." The warehouse building address stuck with me—Harbor Road. The old man had mentioned it specifically, said the vehicle he arrived in was registered somewhere. I found out later that someone had run a license plate check with a man named Eddie. That would be the first real lead in understanding who was actually directing operations. Harbor Road turned out to be the primary distribution hub where artwork from the Kaslan network was temporarily stored before international shipment through legitimate freight channels.

"The last time you saw the kid—do you remember when that was?"

Jerry was quiet for a moment. He lit another cigarette from the one he'd just finished. "Maybe two weeks ago. Three at most. He called for a pickup from his apartment building, and I drove him to the train station. Union Station, the big one downtown. He had a suitcase with him, small one, like a man traveling for a few days. When I helped him load it, I felt something heavy inside—I later remembered him mentioning a small leather-bound ledger with October 15th written on the spine. He was protecting it like it would change everything if the wrong people found it. But the way he moved when he got out of the cab... Detective, I'm telling you, he was leaving. Not for a few days. Leaving. On the drive, he kept repeating something under his breath. 'It's finished. It's done now. He'll have to accept it's finished.' Over and over, like he was working up the courage to believe it himself. Like he'd just crossed a line he couldn't uncross, but that crossing was the only way out of whatever he'd been trapped in."

"Did he say where he was going?"

"No. He just paid the fare and didn't tip—first time in all our rides together that he didn't tip—and got out of the cab like someone walking to their execution. I watched him go through the terminal doors, and I thought, 'That kid's running. That kid's running from something, or toward something, and either way, he's not coming back.'"

"One more thing," Jerry said, reaching back toward his glove box. He produced a folded stub, soft with age and cab grease. "Found it wedged under the seat after he'd gone. Train receipt, northbound, stamped *Harwick Falls*—two stops up the coast from the city terminus. The ticket was dated October 30th, 9:15 AM departure. That's nowhere. That's exactly the kind of nowhere a man chooses when he doesn't want to be found."

"Have you seen anything since? Anything in the news, anything on the radio?"

"No. But I don't really follow what's in the papers anymore. I just drive. I take people from point A to point B and I try not to ask too many questions and I try to stay alive." Jerry stubbed out his cigarette. "But I'll tell you what I know. That old man paying me to forget—that's the kind of thing that happens before something bad. That's the kind of transaction that precedes violence or crime or the kind of human suffering that doesn't make it into the newspapers because the newspapers don't know how to write about it."

"One last thing," Rossi said. "The redhead. Did you ever drive a redhead in connection with this case?"

Jerry thought about it. "There was a woman, maybe three weeks ago, picked up from The Crimson Door. Redhead, like you said. Beautiful in the kind of way that makes you understand why men do stupid things. I didn't drive her specifically with the kid, but I heard her ask for the same address—the residential district, Oak Hill. And she had the same look he had. The same look of someone running."
