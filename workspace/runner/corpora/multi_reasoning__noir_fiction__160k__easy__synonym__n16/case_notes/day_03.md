# Day Three: Digging Deeper

**Date:** November 6, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 6:45 AM - 11:30 PM

---

## Morning After

I don't sleep. Not much anyway. The man's visit sits with me like a bad meal, heavy and wrong. By six-thirty I'm showered and caffeinated, running through scenarios.

The threat is clear enough: stop asking questions or face consequences. In my line of work, threats are just information. They tell you who's scared and what they're scared of. The man came to warn me, which means I'm asking the right questions.

The question is: what's the right answer?

I start by getting a description of Vincent Castellano from the society pages. Twenty-eight years old, educated at private schools, described in one article as "a promising businessman in the import sector." Translation: he's a criminal with good marketing. There are photographs. He's handsome in that way rich boys are, like the world's been soft with him his whole life.

Until Tuesday.

## The Castellano Family Tree

By nine a.m. I'm at the library, going through financial records and property deeds. The Castellano family owns real estate across the city—warehouses in the harbor district, apartments in the downtown area, a nightclub called the Paradise on Seventh.

The Paradise. That's where I need to start.

I also find something interesting: Margaret Hollister's real estate company, Hollister Realty, has sold seven properties to Castellano shell companies over the past three years. The amounts are substantial. The properties are the kind that make good money disappear into legitimate-looking businesses.

Which means Margaret wasn't just running a real estate business. She was laundering money for the Castellano family.

That changes things. That explains why she hired me. It also explains why the man came to my apartment. I'm not just looking for a missing girl anymore. I'm potentially threatening a money laundering operation. And somewhere in this city, there's a ledger dated October 15th that documents every fraudulent transaction, every property flip that was actually a money wash, every connection between Margaret's business and Sinclair's operation. That ledger is the evidence that would destroy them.

## The Morgue

Before I hit the Paradise, I make a stop that I wanted to avoid. The city morgue, gray and cold and smelling like chemicals and bad news. Dr. Raymond, the coroner, is a thin man with kind eyes and the demeanor of someone who's been looking at death long enough to be sad about it.

"Looking for a specific body?" Raymond asks.

I describe Claire Hollister. Young woman, brown hair, probably brought in the last two days.

Raymond checks his ledger. "Nothing matching that description in the past week. Couple of drunk drivers, one drowning, one stabbing. But nothing that fits your girl."

That's good news and bad news. Good news: she's not here. Bad news: she's somewhere, and if she's not in the morgue, she might be somewhere worse than dead.

I ask about Vincent Castellano, just in case.

"That name mean something to you?" Raymond asks carefully.

"It might."

"Then I'll tell you that I haven't seen him. And I'll also tell you that there are certain bodies that come to my morgue and then leave before I finish paperwork. Those are the ones that were important while they were alive."

The message is clear. Some bodies disappear.

## The Paradise

The Paradise nightclub doesn't open until nine p.m., but I know the manager, a woman named Sylvia who's been running clubs in this city since before I was old enough to drink legally. I find her at the Paradise around two o'clock in the afternoon, doing inventory in the back office.

Sylvia is maybe fifty, dressed expensive, the kind of woman who's forgotten more about this city's underworld than most people will ever know. She's also a woman who does favors for people who ask right.

"I'm looking for information about Vincent Castellano," I tell her. "And a girl named Claire Hollister."

Sylvia doesn't look up from her inventory. "That's not information I trade in."

"Someone threatened me about asking questions about them."

Now Sylvia looks up. "Then you already know more than you should."

I sit down across from her. "I'm not looking to hurt anyone. I'm looking to understand what happened to a girl."

Sylvia puts down her pen. "The Castellano kid came in Tuesday night. Brought a girl with him—young, pretty, nervous like she'd never been in a place like this before. He showed her around, introduced her to his father. That was her mistake. The Castellano family operation—gambling, protection, some drug distribution—was small-time compared to Meridian Holdings, but they had access to street-level intelligence that made them useful. Claire's death demonstrated that Meridian Holdings viewed the family as expendable once they had become a liability."

"What do you mean?"

"I mean that Carmine Castellano saw something in that girl that reminded him of someone. Or maybe he saw something that he wanted. I don't know what it was, but whatever it was, it was important enough to kill her for."

There it is. The confirmation.

"When?" I ask.

"The girl was alive when she left the Paradise. But not for long after that. Vincent knew it. He tried to stop them from taking her, and they hit him for his trouble. He's alive, probably, but he knows he's dead. That's almost worse."

"Who took her?"

"A man came in Wednesday morning. Older, well-dressed, the kind of man who nobody says no to. He came looking for the girl. Carmine Castellano tried to play it cool, said the girl was gone, didn't know where. The man didn't believe him. By the time the man left, everyone in the Paradise knew that the Castellano family was in trouble."

"This man," I say. "What did he look like?"

Sylvia gives me a description that matches the man who came to my apartment. Tall, dark-haired, dangerous. She also gives me something else: a name.

"They called him Mr. Sinclair," Sylvia says. "Not his real name, probably. But that's what was used. And I'll tell you something else—Sinclair works for someone bigger. Someone who makes the Castellano family look like street hustlers."

"Who?"

"The kind of person you don't ask questions about," Sylvia says. "The kind of person who has enough money to own half the city and enough power to hide the other half."

## The Mercer Connection

By four o'clock I'm back at the Whitfield Building, taking the elevator up to Mercer & Associates. This time I don't ask politely. This time I walk into Mercer's office like I own it.

Mercer is alone, which is lucky. He looks up from his desk with the expression of a man who's been expecting trouble but hoped it would take longer.

"You know what happened to Claire Hollister," I say, sitting down without waiting to be asked.

"I know what I've been told not to know," Mercer says carefully. "Which is a different thing entirely."

"She's dead."

Mercer doesn't answer. In his silence, I get my confirmation.

"The Castellano family used your law firm to handle their money," I continue. "Claire's mother used your services as well. That's not a coincidence."

"No," Mercer says. "It's not a coincidence. It's the way this city works. Money flows, and lawyers like me make sure it flows through the right channels. I'm not proud of it, but I'm practical about it."

"Someone came to my apartment and told me to stop asking questions."

Mercer's face goes very still. "Then you should stop asking questions."

"Who is Sinclair?"

"I can't tell you that." But Mercer's fear and silence told me enough. Sinclair was someone so powerful that even a lawyer couldn't speak his name aloud without fear. The truth about Sinclair lay in understanding the Kaslan operation—the art theft network that Harvey had been involved with, which distributed stolen goods through channels nobody could trace.

"Because you're legally bound to secrecy, or because you're afraid?"

"Both," Mercer says. "And more importantly, because if you know his name, you're already in danger. And I'm of the opinion that you're a smart man, and a smart man knows when to cut his losses."

I stand up. "You're going to tell me who he is."

"I'm not," Mercer says. "But I'll tell you where to find the answer. Go to the harbor district. There's a warehouse registered to a company called Sinclair Imports. It's on Dock Street, building seven. That's where Claire went. That's where Vincent Castellano went. And that's where you'll find the truth, if you're stupid enough to look."

"Why are you telling me this?"

Mercer leans back in his chair. "Because I defended Claire Hollister's mother in a real estate case three years ago. Because I've been sending money to an orphanage in the girl's name. Because I'm tired of being complicit in people disappearing. And because I know that you're not going to stop, so I might as well point you in a direction that's quick rather than slow."

## The Warehouse

By eight p.m., I'm in the harbor district, watching the warehouse from across the street. It's an old building, probably built twenty years ago, the kind of place that's supposed to be full of imported goods that probably never passed through customs officially.

There are lights on inside. The kind of lights that suggest activity.

I'm about to make my approach when a car pulls up in front of the warehouse. A nice car, black, expensive. The man I've been looking for—Mr. Sinclair—gets out of the driver's seat. He's carrying a briefcase.

I wait, watching.

Ten minutes later, a second figure emerges from the warehouse. It takes me a moment to recognize him because he's changed so much. Vincent Castellano. But not the handsome kid from the society pages. This Vincent is beaten, broken, the kind of broken that comes from knowing your life is over.

Sinclair and Vincent have a conversation I can't hear. Then Sinclair opens the trunk of the car and shows Vincent something. Vincent looks away. Then Sinclair closes the trunk, and Vincent goes back into the warehouse.

I'm tempted to break the trunk open right there, to find out what Sinclair is showing people that makes them cooperate so readily.

But then I remember the man's warning, and I make a decision.

I don't open the trunk. Instead, I note the license plate number. It's a city car, meaning it's registered locally. Traceable.

## Home and Thinking

By midnight I'm back at my apartment, staring at the license plate number and trying to figure out what to do with it.

I have a friend at the DMV named Eddie. Eddie owes me a favor. Eddie can look up a license plate and tell me who owns the car. But if I make that call, if I actually follow this thread, then I'm committed. I'm no longer a private investigator asking questions. I'm a man who's going after someone connected to people important enough to own warehouses full of who-knows-what.

The decent thing would be to call Margaret and tell her what I've found. Tell her that her daughter is dead. Tell her that the man responsible is a person she'll never be able to touch.

But I don't call Margaret.

Instead, I pour a drink and stare out the window at the city lights, all those little fires burning in the darkness, and I wonder how many of them are hiding bodies.

## The Missing Boy

Before I sleep, one more thing nags at me: Vincent Castellano. The kid is alive, at least for now. But he's in the warehouse. That means either he's being held, or he's chosen to stay. Either way, he's a loose end.

And loose ends, in this city, usually get trimmed.

The question is whether I'm going to let that happen or whether I'm going to do something about it.

I know which one I should choose. The decent choice. The safe choice.

I also know which one I'm going to make.

## Notes for Tomorrow

- Call Eddie at DMV—trace the license plate on Sinclair's car
- Find out who owns Sinclair Imports and the warehouse
- Determine if there are other Sinclair-connected properties in the city
- Consider how to approach the warehouse without getting killed
- Decide whether saving Vincent Castellano is worth the risk
- Prepare for the possibility that someone is watching my movements

This case has moved beyond investigation. I'm no longer looking for a missing girl. I'm looking at a conspiracy that involves cops, lawyers, real estate developers, and a criminal organization powerful enough to make people disappear.

The question now is whether I'm looking at the conspiracy or whether the conspiracy is looking at me.

Either way, I think I'm about to find out what Mr. Sinclair really wants.

And I'm getting the feeling it's not going to be pleasant.
