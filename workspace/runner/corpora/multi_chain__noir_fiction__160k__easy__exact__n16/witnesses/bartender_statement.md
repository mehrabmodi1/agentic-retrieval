# Witness Statement: Marcus "Deke" Dekker
## Bartender, The Crimson Door Lounge

**Date:** November 14, 1947  
**Time:** 11:30 PM  
**Location:** The Crimson Door Lounge, 412 Walnut Street, Third Floor  
**Interviewer:** Detective Frank Morello  
**Status:** Voluntary Statement

---

Deke polished a rocks glass with the practiced indifference of a man who'd seen everything and forgotten most of it. The bar was his kingdom, and he ruled it with the quiet authority of someone who knew where every skeleton was buried. His face was weathered like old leather, the lines deep as knife cuts, his eyes pale gray and perpetually tired. The scar tissue around his knuckles told stories he'd never put into words.

"Yeah, I remember the kid," Deke said, sliding me the beer I hadn't ordered but always wanted. "Comes in here maybe twice a week. Always alone. Always sits in that corner booth—the one under the painting of the gambler that some widow donated five years back."

Detective Morello leaned against the bar, the glow of his cigarette casting shadows across his angular face. The lounge smelled of stale whiskey, spilled bourbon, and the kind of desperation that money can't quite wash away. The jukebox played something mournful—a woman's voice singing about love that died before it was born.

"Tell me about last Thursday," Morello said.

"Thursday." Deke frowned, thinking. His fingers moved methodically over the glass. "That'd be the day he came in around eight o'clock. Real nervous, you know? The kind of nervous where a guy keeps looking at the door, checking his watch like time's running out on some kind of promise. He ordered a rye, straight, didn't even touch it for the first ten minutes. Just sat there staring at it like it might offer him an answer to something."

"Did he meet anyone that night?"

"Not while I was watching. But around nine, maybe nine-fifteen, this dame came in. Redhead. Beautiful in that dangerous way—the kind of beauty that walks in a room and makes every man remember he's got something to lose. She wore a coat, green, expensive looking. Fur collar. She didn't see the kid at first, just ordered a martini and sat at the bar. But then..." Deke paused, his hands stilling on the glass. "Then she turned. Maybe thirty seconds passed, both of them looking at each other like they'd been waiting their whole lives to meet, and it had finally happened. Or maybe like they'd met before and neither of them wanted to remember why."

"They speak?"

"Not right then. But when I delivered his second drink—he'd finally moved to the one he'd ordered earlier—she paid her tab and walked over. I couldn't hear much. The jukebox was playing loud. But I saw her lean in close, saw her lips moving. The kid's face went white. I mean, I've seen men get bad news in this place. I've poured drinks for guys who just lost their fortunes, their wives, their freedom. But I'd never seen a face drain of color like that. Like someone had just told him the war wasn't over, it was just starting."

"Did they leave together?"

Deke shook his head slowly. "That's the thing. She took his hand—quick gesture, like she was afraid someone might see—squeezed it, and left. Just like that. Walked out like she'd never been there. The kid sat there for another twenty minutes, didn't move, didn't drink anything else. Then he left too. Didn't tip. First time in maybe six months he didn't leave a tip."

"You ever see him with anyone else? Any regular companions?"

"Once. Maybe two weeks ago. There was an older man, maybe late fifties, heavy-set type with white hair and the kind of face that'd probably been handsome twenty years ago before whatever was eating him at the inside did its work. They sat together, didn't talk much. But I noticed the old guy slid something across the table—looked like an envelope. White envelope. The kid put it in his jacket without looking at it. Like he'd been expecting it but didn't want to acknowledge the expectation."

Morello tapped ash into the tray I'd placed on the bar without thinking. "Anything else unusual about their interaction?"

"Yeah, actually." Deke's pale eyes fixed on something in the middle distance, seeing something that happened ten days earlier. "When the old guy left, he put his hand on the kid's shoulder. There was something in the gesture—something almost paternal, you know? But also something like a warning. Like a man putting his hand on a horse's neck before the race, trying to tell it to run but also knowing the horse might not come back. Before he stood up, the old man grabbed the kid's wrist—not rough, but firm—and I saw his mouth move. Just a few words. The kid nodded, eyes down. Whatever was said in that moment, it wasn't a request. It was a reminder. A condition. When the kid stood up, his hands were shaking."

"He leaned down and said something before he took his hand away. Bar was quiet that night, and I caught a few words clear enough—'the Starlight Motor Inn, room fourteen, the key's under the mat.' Said it like giving directions, real matter-of-fact. The kid nodded once, didn't write it down. The kind of thing a man memorizes because he knows he'll need it."

"The envelope. Did you ever see the kid open it?"

""Didn't stick around to find out. I've learned that the less I know about what happens in the corners and the booths, the longer I keep my liquor license and my teeth." 

Off the record, Deke added: "Jerry Salvatore drove the kid places. Cab driver. He'll tell you where the kid went after leaving here. Check with the cab company on Industrial Avenue—that's the real trail."" Deke set down the glass he'd been polishing. It caught the light, gleamed like a crystal ball that sees everything but tells nothing. "But I'll tell you something I know about people, Detective. The ones who come in here with envelopes and nervous eyes and beautiful women who disappear—those people don't usually end up in stories with happy endings."

"The redhead," Morello said. "You ever see her before?"

"Once. Just once. Two months ago, maybe longer. She came in with a different guy—a suit, the expensive kind that costs more than I make in a month. They had champagne. Real champagne, not the rot-gut stuff we pass off to the tourists. They talked for maybe an hour, and then she cried. Just a single tear running down her cheek, but it was the kind of tear that means something got broken that can't be fixed. The guy left money on the table and walked out. She stayed for another drink, alone, staring at nothing."

"You get a name? Anything?"

Deke smiled sadly. "I learned a long time ago not to ask names. Names attach people to places, and places attached to people get found when they shouldn't be found. I keep their secrets because their secrets keep them coming back. That's how this business works."

Morello stubbed out his cigarette and ordered a water. "Anyone threatening the kid? Anyone who seemed to want him gone?"

"No threats. But there was tension around him, you know? The kind of tension that precedes something. Like when you can smell rain coming even though the sky is still clear. The kid had that kind of aura—like destiny was circling, getting closer." Deke pulled out a bottle, poured carefully. "I've been tending bar for thirty years, Detective. I've learned that the kids who come in looking sad and lost don't usually leave the same way. They leave changed, or they don't leave at all."
