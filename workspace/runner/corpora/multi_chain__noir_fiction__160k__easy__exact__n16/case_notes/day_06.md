# Day Six: The Pressure Mounts

**Date:** November 9, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 8:00 AM - 4:00 AM (next day)

---

## Morning After

I don't die in the night, which is either good news or means Sinclair's taking his time. I wake up to my phone ringing at eight a.m.

It's Detective Morris, the cop who was friendly enough to tell me that the Castellano family was marked for trouble.

"You stirred something up," Morris says without preamble.

"Someone needed to stir something."

"Yeah, well, you stirred it in the wrong direction. Sinclair filed a complaint against you. Says you broke into a warehouse, threatened him with a gun, and let a fugitive escape. He's providing evidence."

"What kind of evidence?"

"The kind that puts you in the warehouse. Security cameras, apparently. Sinclair's got everything documented. He's got you on tape, clear as day, committing crimes."

This is bad. This is worse than bad. This is the kind of bad that gets me arrested.

"There's more," Morris continues. "Sinclair's pressing charges. Attempted murder, breaking and entering, conspiracy, the works. By tomorrow morning there'll be a warrant for your arrest."

"He set me up," I say.

"Of course he set you up. That's what men like Sinclair do. They wait for you to make a move, then they use that move against you. You did exactly what he wanted you to do."

## The Setup

It hits me all at once. Sinclair could have had me killed in that warehouse. Could have easily. I was pointing a gun at him, which gave him legal justification to shoot me in self-defense. But instead, he let me leave.

Because he wanted me to leave. He wanted me to go to the police looking like a lunatic who'd broken into a warehouse and threatened him with a gun. He wanted me to become the criminal and himself the victim.

And I walked right into it.

By noon, I'm talking to Helen Crawford, my lawyer. Helen's face is very serious.

"You need to leave the city," Helen says. "Immediately. You can't stay here."

"If I run, it makes me look guilty."

"You are guilty. You did break into that warehouse. You did point a gun at him. You did let a suspect escape. Legally, Sinclair has you trapped. If you stay here, you get arrested, you get tried, and the evidence against you—the security footage, your own admission that you were there—will convict you."

"What about the evidence I gave you? The documentation about the money laundering?"

"That's not enough. That shows Sinclair's criminal activity, but it doesn't prove he murdered Claire Hollister. Without a body, without physical evidence, without witnesses willing to testify, you don't have a murder case. You just have a man accused of financial crimes, which he'll beat using expensive lawyers."

Helen is right. I can see it clearly. Sinclair has turned this around. Now I'm the criminal, and he's the businessman protecting his warehouse from a lunatic investigator.

## The Reporter

Helen hands me a business card. "David Chen. Reporter at the Chronicle. I called him this morning, told him about the situation. He wants to talk to you."

By two p.m., I'm meeting David Chen at a coffee shop far enough from downtown to not be obvious. Chen is exactly what I expected: mid-fifties, the look of a man who's spent his career chasing stories nobody else wanted to publish.

"I've been waiting for someone to come forward about Victor Ashford for three years," Chen tells me. "I've got fragments. I've got suspicions. But I don't have the proof."

"I've got documentation," I tell Chen, and I hand him the envelope that Mercer gave me. "This is money laundering connected to Sinclair's imports and Ashford's operation. Wire transfers routed through shell corporations in three countries, with dates that align precisely with Harvey's involvement in the art theft network—he was facilitating sales and moving proceeds through the import-export infrastructure."

Chen goes through the documents carefully. His face doesn't change, but his hands are steady. This is a man who knows how to handle evidence.

"This is good," Chen says. "This is really good. But it's not enough by itself to run a story that'll stick. The lawyers will tear this apart."

"The girl," I tell Chen. "Claire Hollister. She knew something about the operation. She was killed to keep her quiet. She's buried somewhere in the harbor district, probably. If you can find her body—"

"Finding a body in a city of a million people is like finding a specific grain of sand on a beach," Chen interrupts. "I can try, but I can't promise anything. What I can do is run the financial story. I can connect Sinclair to Ashford, I can show money movements, I can make enough noise that someone official is forced to investigate."

"How long?"

"Two weeks. I need two weeks to verify everything and talk to sources who are willing to go on record."

I don't have two weeks. I have until they arrest me.

## The Trap Tightens

By five p.m., my building superintendent knocks on my door. Two cops behind him. One of them has a warrant.

I've been arrested before, but never for something serious. This is serious. This is the kind of serious that comes with actual consequences.

They read me my rights. They take me downtown. They put me in a cell with three other men, one of whom is currently being sick in the corner, and I have several hours to think about how badly I've miscalculated.

## The Interrogation

By midnight, I'm in an interrogation room with Detective Krantz, who's clearly been given the job of nailing me. Krantz is methodical. He walks me through everything: entering the warehouse, confronting Sinclair, pulling a gun, letting Vincent Castellano escape.

"I'm trying to understand your motive," Krantz says. "Why do you break into a man's warehouse? Why do you point a gun at him?"

I don't say anything. My lawyer, Helen, is on her way, and until she gets here, the smartest thing I can do is be very quiet.

"Sinclair's willing to drop the charges if you'll sign a statement saying you were acting on your own, that you have no evidence of criminal activity by any party named Sinclair, and that you were mistaken about the nature of his business."

"I'm waiting for my lawyer."

"Your lawyer can't help you. The only thing that can help you is cooperating with us. Sinclair's offered you a way out. Take it."

## The New Information

Helen arrives at two a.m., and the first thing she does is speak to me alone.

"I've been contacted by Thomas Mercer," Helen tells me quietly. "He's willing to testify. He's willing to provide evidence about the money laundering, about Sinclair's orders to eliminate Claire Hollister, about the entire operation."

"Why?"

"Because he's dying."

That's not what I expected to hear.

"Mercer has cancer," Helen continues. "Six months, maybe less. He went to the hospital today for a checkup, and they told him it's terminal. Since he's dying anyway, he's decided that his conscience is worth more than his comfort. He's willing to testify, knowing it will probably get him killed faster."

"That's suicide."

"Probably. But at least it's his choice."

With Mercer's testimony, the case changes. Suddenly there's a witness. Someone who was actually in the room when Sinclair ordered the removal of the girl. Someone who can connect the money to the crime.

Mercer's testimony, combined with Chen's story about the money laundering, combined with the evidence I've gathered—it's not enough to prove murder, but it's enough to open an investigation.

And an investigation, in a city like this, is sometimes all you need.

## The Deal

Helen goes back to the cops and negotiates. In exchange for my cooperation, in exchange for me testifying about what I saw in the warehouse, they agree not to press charges on the breaking and entering. They're dropping the attempted murder charge because I never actually fired the gun.

What's left is still serious, but it's survivable. What's left can be beaten if Mercer and I both testify.

I sign the agreement. I'm released on my own recognizance with the condition that I don't leave the city.

As I'm being escorted out of the station, one of the cops leans in close.

"Sinclair's going to know about the deal," the cop says quietly. "He's going to know that you're going to testify. And he's going to try to stop you before you get in front of a jury."

"I know."

"So you should know that you're walking out of this building into the city, and there are people in the city who want you dead."

## That Night

I don't go home. I go to a hotel in the South End under a false name. I pay cash. I don't use the phone. I don't contact anyone except Helen.

Helen tells me that David Chen is accelerating his story. It's going to run in two days, not two weeks. It's going to expose Ashford, Sinclair, and the money laundering operation. It's going to force the authorities to take action.

Chen says he's had a breakthrough. Someone finally willing to talk. A former Sinclair employee who's decided that loyalty's less valuable than truth.

This person has also given Chen something else: the location of Claire Hollister's body.

## The Location

Early morning on November 10, the police find Claire's body in the basement of an abandoned building on Harbor Avenue, two blocks from Sinclair's warehouse. She's been there since Wednesday night. She's been there while her mother's been going crazy, while I've been chasing leads, while Sinclair's been trying to make me disappear.

The body is identified through dental records. It's definitely Claire Hollister. The cause of death is still being determined, but the police are treating it as homicide.

This changes everything. This turns a financial crime into a murder case. This makes Sinclair vulnerable in ways that money and power might not protect him from.

## The Cost

I'm sitting in my hotel room reading the preliminary report about Claire's death when I get a phone call. It's Helen.

"Mercer was hit by a car this morning," Helen says. "He's in Saint Michael's Hospital. Critical condition. It was probably not an accident."

Mercer's testimony, which was supposed to be the keystone of this case, is now potentially gone. If he dies, if he can't testify, then we lose his direct knowledge of Sinclair's orders. Before the accident, Mercer left word with a nurse: "Check the paradise club manager. She knows about Vincent Castellano and the girl. That's where it all connects."

But the body changes things. The body is evidence. The body is proof that a crime happened, that something real and terrible occurred that can't just be explained away by security footage and security measures.

## Notes for Tomorrow

- David Chen's story runs tomorrow morning
- Police will begin an official investigation into Sinclair and Ashford
- Mercer's recovery is uncertain but his testimony is no longer the only piece of the puzzle
- Expect Sinclair to make a move before the story breaks
- Prepare for the possibility that being a witness means I'm still in danger
- Consider whether leaving the city is still a viable option (probably not)

I stand at the window of my hotel room and look out at the city lights. The same lights I was looking at a week ago when Margaret Hollister walked into my office with a missing girl and a mother's fear.

A week. Seven days.

In that week, someone murdered a girl and dumped her body in the basement of an abandoned building. In that week, a man offered me money to go away. In that week, I broke into a warehouse and pointed a gun at a man and let a kid escape.

In that week, I made everything worse and somehow, by making everything worse, I made it better.

Tomorrow the story breaks. Tomorrow the city's machine has to acknowledge that something happened that can't be ignored.

Tomorrow Sinclair finds out that killing the girl didn't make the problem go away. It just delayed it.

I wonder how he's going to respond to that knowledge.

I'm afraid I'm about to find out.
