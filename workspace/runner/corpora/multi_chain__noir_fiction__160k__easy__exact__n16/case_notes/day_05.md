# Day Five: The Point of No Return

**Date:** November 8, 1947  
**Time Entries:** 5:30 AM - 2:20 AM (next day)

---

## The Decision

I wake up at five-thirty because I never actually slept. Just lay in bed thinking, which is worse than being awake because at least being awake lets you move. By six I've made a decision.

I'm not taking Sinclair's deal.

This isn't because I'm brave or moral or righteous. It's because I've spent three days learning the names of the dead, and I know that if I pretend I don't know them, they stay dead. If I actually say their names, if I write them down, if I make them impossible to ignore, then maybe they get a little bit of justice.

Maybe. That's all I've got.

I make a list:
- Claire Anne Hollister, 23, real estate secretary, killed Wednesday night
- Vincent Castellano, 28, accidental witness to something he shouldn't have seen
- Unknown others, disappeared by the same mechanism

Three names. At minimum three names. Probably more.

## The Insurance

By eight a.m., I'm at a lawyer's office. Not Mercer. Someone different. A woman named Helen Crawford who's been handling criminal defense cases for twenty years and who knows better than to ask too many questions but smart enough to listen to the ones that matter.

"I have information about a murder," I tell Helen. "I need to protect it in case something happens to me."

Helen doesn't say anything, just gestures for me to continue.

I spend two hours writing out everything I know. Names, dates, locations, theories, the connection to Margaret Hollister's money laundering, the warehouse on Dock Street, Sinclair, Ashford, all of it. I write it in narrative form, the kind that would make sense to a judge if it ever had to be read in court.

When I'm done, Helen reads it carefully.

"This is circumstantial," Helen says. "But it's solid circumstantial. It's enough to open an investigation. It's enough to make trouble for powerful people."

"I need you to hold this document. If something happens to me, I need you to give it to someone. Someone trustworthy. Someone who won't be bought."

"There's someone," Helen says. "A reporter named David Chen at the Chronicle. He's been trying to expose corruption in this city for ten years. He's beaten two bribery attempts and survived a car accident that was probably not an accident. If you want the truth to come out, Chen's your man."

I have Helen put the document in an envelope, sealed, with strict instructions that if I don't check in with her once a week, she's to deliver it to Chen immediately.

Helen charges me five hundred dollars, which is expensive but not expensive enough to make me flinch.

## The Hospital Visit

By ten a.m., I'm back at Saint Michael's Hospital, looking for Patricia. I find her on her break, smoking a cigarette in the staff lounge.

"The man who picked up James Patterson," I say. "The one who helped him into the car. Did you happen to notice which direction they went?"

Patricia thinks. "North. They headed north toward the financial district."

North. Toward Sinclair's offices, probably. Toward wherever rich men take broken people to make sure they disappear properly.

Patricia had left me one more piece before I left the hospital: "The man who picked him up mentioned checking the paradise nightclub. That's where the girl was last seen—that's your next thread."

"One more thing," Patricia says. "The man left something behind. A business card that fell out of his pocket when he was helping the patient. I picked it up."

Patricia hands me the card. It's elegant, expensive, minimalist. Just a name and a phone number.

"Charles Sinclair - Private Consulting Services"

I pocket the card. This is the closest thing to physical evidence I have, which is almost worthless in a court of law but might be useful in other ways.

## The Lawyer Again

By afternoon, I'm back at Mercer & Associates, and this time I don't wait for the receptionist. I walk right into Mercer's office.

Mercer doesn't look surprised. He looks like a man who's been expecting his past to catch up with him.

"I know what you did," I tell him. "You represented the Castellano family knowing that they were connected to Sinclair. You helped launder money knowing it was dirty. You did it because someone had leverage on you, or because you were paid well, or both."

"If you know what I did, then you know there's nothing you can do about it. I'm a lawyer. Attorney-client privilege protects me."

"Privilege doesn't protect you from murder. Privilege doesn't protect you from being an accessory to the fact."

Mercer leans back in his chair. "Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I need a witness. I need someone who can testify about what Sinclair did. And I think you're feeling guilty enough that you might be willing to do it."

"If I testify, Sinclair will kill me."

"Probably," I agree. "But your name's already in this file. And I've made arrangements that if anything happens to me, that file goes to the newspapers. So whether you testify or not, you're already in danger. You might as well make the danger mean something."

Mercer stands up and goes to the window. "You're a fool," he says. "You know that, right? You're a fool with a dead girl and no way to prove it, and you're pushing against people who can make you disappear."

"I know."

"And you're going to do it anyway."

"I'm going to do it anyway."

Mercer turns back to me. "Then I can't help you. If I testify, I die. If I don't testify, I live but I hate myself. I've chosen to live with the hate."

He opens a drawer in his desk and pulls out an envelope. "But I can give you this. It's copies of every transaction between Sinclair's companies and Margaret Hollister's business. It's documentation that connects Victor Ashford to the money pipeline. It's not going to be enough to convict anyone, but it's enough to make a lot of noise. If you make noise loud enough, maybe some of it sticks."

I take the envelope. It's thick. It's real.

"Why?" I ask Mercer.

"Because I've been representing the guilty for twenty years, and I'm tired of it. Because Claire Hollister shouldn't be dead, and the least I can do is make sure her death meant something. And because you're right—I'm going to die anyway. Might as well make it count."

## The Warehouse

By six p.m., I've done something probably very stupid. I've borrowed a gun from a friend named Marcus who works in security. It's a .38, heavy, the kind of gun that doesn't offer solutions so much as different ways to make problems permanent.

I drive to the warehouse on Dock Street. I don't have a plan. Plans are for people who have time and resources. I have a deadline and a manila envelope full of evidence.

The warehouse is exactly as I left it. Loading dock facing the street, side entrance, no visible guards. But that doesn't mean there aren't guards. It just means they're inside.

I park the car three blocks away and approach on foot.

The side entrance is locked, but the lock is old, the kind that yields to certain persuasions. I get inside without triggering alarms, which either means there are no alarms or they're being ignored.

The inside of the warehouse is what you'd expect: rows of crates, the smell of import and possibility, the sense of a place designed to move things across borders without too many questions.

But in the back office, there are people.

I hear voices before I see them. One belongs to Sinclair, smooth and controlled. The other is younger, more desperate. Vincent Castellano.

"You understand what's going to happen," Sinclair is saying. "You're going to disappear. Voluntarily. You're going to leave the city and never come back. And your family is going to understand that if you talk, they all disappear with you."

"I want to see her," Vincent says. "I want to see Claire."

"Claire is gone. She was a complication that had to be resolved. You need to accept that and move on."

"How much money are you giving me?" Vincent asks.

"Fifty thousand dollars. Cash. In unmarked bills. Enough to start over somewhere else. Enough to build a new life if you're smart about it."

I'm about to back out and call the police when Vincent says something that makes me stop.

"I have photographs," Vincent says. "Photographs from Tuesday night. At the hotel. Before you took her. I have photographs that show everything."

In the silence that follows, I pull the gun from my pocket.

Sinclair's voice, when he speaks, is very still. "You have photographs."

"Yes. And if something happens to me, or to my family, they go to the newspapers. I'm not stupid. I know you're going to kill me anyway, but I want insurance."

"Show me the photographs," Sinclair says.

I see this as my cue. I push open the office door and point the gun at Sinclair.

"Nobody move," I say.

Sinclair doesn't move. He doesn't even look surprised. Vincent does, though. Vincent looks shocked, which is reasonable considering he didn't know there was a third party in his negotiation for his life.

"You," Sinclair says, looking at me with something like amusement. "The detective. You chose to take the hard road."

"Claire Hollister," I say. "What did you do with her body?"

"That's not relevant anymore. The girl is gone. She's dead. The only question now is whether you're going to join her or whether you're going to walk out that door and never come back."

The gun feels very heavy in my hand.

"I have evidence," I tell Sinclair. "Evidence about your money laundering, your import business, all of it. I've given it to a lawyer. If anything happens to me, it goes to the newspapers."

"Then you've guaranteed that something will happen to you," Sinclair says calmly. "Because now you're not a problem that goes away quietly. Now you're a liability that needs to be addressed. You've just assured yourself of death, detective. The only question is how long it takes."

I realize I've made a tactical error. I've announced my insurance policy, which means I've just made my death inevitable instead of merely likely.

Before I can process this, Vincent makes his move. He throws something at Sinclair—the photographs, maybe—and bolts for the door. Sinclair doesn't follow him. Instead, he raises his hands and smiles.

"You're going to kill me?" Sinclair asks.

"I'm going to try."

"No, you're not. Because if you kill me, you become a murderer. And murderers get caught in this city, regardless of how powerful they are. And you don't have the power, detective. You have a gun and a moral code and neither of those things matter."

He's right. I'm standing in a warehouse with a gun pointed at a man, and what I want to do is make him pay for Claire Hollister. But making him pay means I get the chair or spend the rest of my life in a cage.

I lower the gun.

"Where's Vincent going?" I ask.

"Wherever kids like that go when they run. Somewhere else. Somewhere they think they can hide. He won't make it far, but he'll make it far enough to be someone else's problem."

I back out of the office, keeping the gun trained on Sinclair, and I see Vincent ahead of me, running toward the exit.

"Come on!" I shout at him, and Vincent doesn't need to be told twice.

We make it out of the warehouse. We make it to my car. We make it two miles before Vincent tells me to pull over.

"I can't go with you," Vincent says. "I'll lead them to you. But I want to thank you, detective. I want you to know that whatever happens to me, it's better than the alternative."

He takes the fifty thousand dollars in cash that Sinclair gave him—which is still in Vincent's pocket—and he disappears into the darkness of the city.

I never see Vincent Castellano again. But later, I find out he makes it as far as Mexico. Whether he actually made it beyond that, I never learn.

## The Ride Home

Driving back to my apartment, I realize that I've accomplished nothing and everything at the same time. I haven't arrested anyone. I haven't recovered Claire's body. I haven't stopped Sinclair or Ashford or whoever's above them.

But I have made noise. And noise, in a city built on silence, is its own kind of power.

## Notes for Tomorrow

- Expect immediate retaliation from Sinclair
- Document everything for the police (though it probably won't matter)
- Decide whether to go public with what I know
- Consider whether leaving the city is the smart move (it probably is)
- Wait for Helen's contact at the Chronicle to reach out
- Figure out why I'm still alive and whether that's about to change

I'm sitting in my apartment at three in the morning, and I realize that I've crossed a line I can't uncross. I've forced Sinclair's hand. I've made it so that he can't ignore me anymore. I've made myself into a problem that requires a solution.

And the solution, for men like Sinclair, is always the same.

I pour another drink and wait to see who comes through the door.
