# Chapter 4: The Apartment

Catherine Halstead's apartment was in a six-story walk-up in the residential district, the kind of building that had aspirations of elegance but had settled for respectability. The super was a woman named Dvorak with gray hair and sharp eyes that missed nothing. I showed her a fifty-dollar bill and told her I was assisting the family with a private matter, and she let me in without asking too many questions.

The apartment was on the fourth floor—small, maybe two rooms and a kitchen, but carefully decorated with the taste of someone who cared about her surroundings. Books were everywhere: on shelves, on the nightstand, in careful stacks on the floor. There were framed photographs—Catherine with her parents, with her three friends, with people I didn't recognize. There was a small painting of the waterfront, possibly her own work. The place told a story of a careful, thoughtful person who spent a lot of time with words and art and ideas.

I started with her desk, a small writing surface by the window. There were bills, paid and organized. There were personal letters, held together with a rubber band—correspondence with her mother, mostly. There was a journal, leather-bound and locked with a small key, which I found in the nightstand drawer along with condoms and some minor jewelry.

I opened the journal with the kind of guilt that came from invading someone's private thoughts, but this wasn't the time for squeamishness.

The entries dated back six months. Most of it was the usual journal material—reflections on books she was reading, thoughts about her work at the cultural foundation, observations about her friends. The handwriting was careful and thoughtful. The person who wrote these entries was intelligent and self-aware in a way that suggested she understood her own nature and wasn't particularly happy about some of it.

Then, about three months ago, the tone changed.

*"Met someone today. Can't write his name yet—feels too real if I write it down. But he's unlike anyone I've met before. Successful, confident, but there's something sad about him. Like he's carrying something that's too heavy. When he looks at me, I feel like he's really seeing me, not just looking at me. It's intoxicating and terrifying in equal measure."*

Over the next few weeks, the entries became more frequent and more intimate, though Catherine was careful never to write her lover's name. She referred to him as "H" in later entries.

*"H says I need to be more careful. Says the city isn't safe, especially for someone as beautiful and vulnerable as me. He's being protective, which I should probably find annoying, but instead I find it comforting. Is that weakness? Or is it just love? I can't tell anymore."*

*"H introduced me to the world of high finance today. Took me to a restaurant I'd never have afforded on my own. Introduced me to his business associates. I felt out of place, dressed too plainly, not sophisticated enough. But H made sure everyone knew I was with him, that I mattered to him. That made all the difference."*

Then, about three weeks ago—precisely when the landlady observed the mysterious visitor ascending to Harvey's apartment—something shifted in Catherine's entries. The entries became worried and introspective.

*"H is different lately. Distracted. When I ask him what's wrong, he changes the subject or holds me without answering. I think something is happening in his business. Something serious. But he won't tell me what it is. He says it's better if I don't know. Says it's to protect me. But it feels like being kept in the dark. How can I help him if I don't understand what's happening?"*

*"Started noticing H having phone conversations late at night. He goes into the other room, speaks quietly, speaks urgently. When he comes back, he kisses my forehead and tells me he loves me, but there's distance in it. Like his mind is somewhere else, somewhere he's afraid to let me follow."*

The most recent entry was dated Monday—two days before she disappeared.

*"I told H today that I can't do this anymore—can't be with someone who won't let me in, won't share his burden. He became very still, very controlled. Said, 'You know too much already, Catherine. And loving me is already dangerous. Please don't ask for more.' Then he left. I don't know if he's coming back. I don't know if I want him to. Everything inside me is pulled in two directions."*

I closed the journal and sat on the bed, thinking. Catherine Halstead had been involved with someone—someone powerful, someone worried, someone who'd warned her about danger. And then she'd gone walking alone at night, and the next thing anyone knew, she was gone and there was a kidnapping note.

It was possible that her mysterious lover had taken her. It was possible that someone had taken her to get to him. It was possible that Catherine knew too much about something illegal or compromising, and this was a warning to keep quiet.

Or it was possible that I was overthinking this, and the answer was simpler than it appeared.

I was interrupted by a knock on the door. I turned to look at the exit, measuring distances, calculating whether I could get out without being seen. Then the door opened anyway.

The man in the doorway was police. I could tell by the way he carried the badge, the way his hand rested near his waist, the way he looked at me like I was a problem that needed solving. He was broad-shouldered, with dark hair starting to gray at the temples and a face that had seen worse things than it should have.

"Lieutenant Marcus Webb, Police Detective Bureau," he said, showing the badge. "And you are?"

"Jack Shepherd. Private investigator." I kept my hands visible and my voice calm. "I was hired by Richard Halstead to look into his daughter's disappearance."

Webb's expression didn't change, but something behind his eyes went harder. "Was he now. Interesting, because the Halstead family called us about an hour ago. Said his daughter was missing but asked us not to pursue it actively. Said they wanted to keep it private while they handled it through other channels. Didn't mention hiring a private investigator."

"Maybe they didn't want to tell you. Maybe they were following instructions from the people who took her."

"That would be kidnapping, wouldn't it? Federal crime. Something the police department is better equipped to handle than some two-bit private eye working out of a office that probably smells like spilled whiskey and broken dreams."

I'd dealt with cops like Webb before. The honest ones were the most dangerous because they didn't have to pretend they were following the rules—they actually believed in them. "I'm cooperating with the family's wishes. If they want to involve the police, they will."

"That's where you're wrong." Webb stepped into the apartment and closed the door behind him. "This is a kidnapping. This is a federal crime. And this is my case now, whether the family likes it or not. Which means you need to step back and let professionals handle it."

"And if stepping back means Catherine gets hurt?"

Webb moved toward me, and I could see the tension in his shoulders. "If Catherine gets hurt, it'll be because someone decided to make things complicated instead of following protocol. You know what kidnappers do when they find out someone's hired a private investigator? They get nervous. And nervous people make mistakes. Nervous people hurt their hostages."

He was right, and I hated that he was right. But I was also right, and he hated that too.

"Look," I said, trying to lower the temperature. "I'm not trying to interfere with police work. I'm trying to help a terrified father find his daughter. If the police are involved now, I'll step back. But if Halstead wanted the police involved, he would have called you from the beginning. He didn't. He called me. That tells me something about what he's afraid of."

Webb looked at the journal in my hand, then back at my face. "That private property?"

"Yes."

"Give it to me."

"It's not evidence. It's a girl's diary."

"Give it to me, Shepherd, or I'll arrest you for breaking and entering and interfering with a police investigation."

I handed him the journal. He took it with the careful precision of a man handling evidence, but his eyes read the conflicted look on my face, and I saw the faintest smile at the corner of his mouth. He knew I was holding back information. He also knew he couldn't prove it.

"Here's how this is going to go," Webb said, tucking the journal under his arm. "You're going to stop investigating. You're going to go back to your office and wait for your client to call and tell you the case is closed. If I find out you're still poking around in this, I'm going to make sure you lose your license. Understood?"

"Crystal clear."

"And Shepherd? If anything happens to Catherine Halstead because you made things complicated, I'm going to make sure the entire city knows whose fault it was."

He walked out of the apartment without waiting for a response. I heard his footsteps descending the stairs, heard the street door slam below.

I stood in Catherine's apartment for a long moment, surrounded by her books and her carefully decorated walls and the artifacts of a life that had been interrupted. Webb was right that I'd made things more complicated. But he was wrong if he thought I was going to stop looking.

The investigation had just become two cases instead of one: finding Catherine Halstead, and figuring out who "H" was and what kind of trouble he represented.

And somewhere in that trouble was the answer to everything.
