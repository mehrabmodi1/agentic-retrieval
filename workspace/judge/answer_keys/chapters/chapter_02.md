
"You look like you haven't slept," I said.

"I haven't." Halstead's hands were shaking slightly as he lifted the cup to his lips. He drank it anyway, not tasting it. "I've been thinking about Catherine. About all the things I should have done differently. All the times I was too busy, too focused on business. I would give everything I have to have those moments back."

That was the part people didn't understand about kidnapping. It wasn't just about the victim—it was a mirror held up to every choice the family had made, every moment they'd taken for granted. It was guilt crystallized into terror.

"Tell me about Catherine. Not the official version—the real one."

Halstead stared into his coffee like it might contain answers. "Catherine is brilliant. Not just intelligent—brilliant. She graduated from university at twenty with advanced degrees in literature and philosophy, both honors. Wanted to be a writer, actually. But when she came back to the city, she started working for the cultural foundation downtown, helping organize exhibitions and museum programs. Modest salary, not what her education could have gotten her, but work that mattered to her."

"Does she have any enemies? Anyone who might want to hurt her?"

"No. Catherine is gentle, thoughtful. She volunteers at a women's shelter on weekends. She gives money to charities. She's one of the few good things in this city, Mr. Shepherd, and I—" His voice cracked. He steadied it with effort. "I'm terrified that her goodness is what's gotten her killed."

I let that hang there for a moment. There was wisdom in what he said. Good people in a bad city were always at risk. It was like being a lamb in a room full of wolves who hadn't eaten in weeks.

"Tell me about the package. I need details."

He pulled out a manila envelope and slid it across the table. Inside was a Polaroid photograph, still fresh enough that I could smell the chemicals. It showed a young woman—blonde hair, green eyes, the kind of face that suggested her father was right about at least one thing. She was sitting on a bare wooden chair in what looked like a basement or warehouse. Her hands were folded in her lap. Her eyes were wide with fear.

And behind her, barely visible in the shadows, was another figure. Just enough to know someone was there. Just enough to know they were watching her.

I handed the photo back before my face could show what I was thinking. "The letter?"

Halstead produced it—typed on plain paper, words cut from newsprint like something out of a pulp novel. It said exactly what he'd told me: *Don't call the police. We'll be in touch. Act normally. Act like nothing's happened.*

"How many people know that Catherine is missing?"

"Just her mother. And my business partner, though I didn't tell him directly—he called wondering why Catherine hadn't come to her mother's birthday dinner on Wednesday. I had to explain." Halstead leaned forward, and I could see the plea in his eyes. "You have to find her before they contact me again. You have to find her before they ask for something I can't give them. The men I've dealt with—they're not acting independently. There's a structure, a hierarchy. Someone above Sinclair, someone orchestrating these operations. Someone who makes decisions that people like Sinclair execute. That's the real power in this city."

Halstead's hands clenched. "There was a man named Harvey. He came to my attention because I realized he worked for the same organization that took Catherine. He was low-level, but he had knowledge. The police have been investigating him, but what they don't understand is that Harvey is their only possible witness to the upper structure. If Harvey vanishes before he can be questioned, the entire operation stays insulated."
