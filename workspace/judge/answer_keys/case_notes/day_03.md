
"I mean that Carmine Castellano saw something in that girl that reminded him of someone. Or maybe he saw something that he wanted. I don't know what it was, but whatever it was, it was important enough to kill her for."

There it is. The confirmation.

"When?" I ask.

"The girl was alive when she left the Paradise. But not for long after that. Vincent knew it. He tried to stop them from taking her, and they hit him for his trouble. He's alive, probably, but he knows he's dead. That's almost worse."

"Who took her?"

"A man came in Wednesday morning. Older, well-dressed, the kind of man who nobody says no to. He came looking for the girl. Carmine Castellano tried to play it cool, said the girl was gone, didn't know where. The man didn't believe him. By the time the man left, everyone in the Paradise knew that the Castellano family was in trouble."

"This man," I say. "What did he look like?"

Sylvia gives me a description that matches the man who came to my apartment. Tall, dark-haired, dangerous. She also gives me something else: a name.

"They called him Mr. Sinclair," Sylvia says. "Not his real name, probably. But that's what was used. And I'll tell you something else—Sinclair works for someone bigger. Someone who makes the Castellano family look like street hustlers."

"Who?"

"The kind of person you don't ask questions about," Sylvia says. "The kind of person who has enough money to own half the city and enough power to hide the other half."

## The Harvey Discovery

In my investigation of the Kaslan connection, I found references to a man named James Harvey in the files Mercer surrendered. Harvey appeared in financial records, in transaction logs, always as a facilitator. He moved things. Arranged things. Made things disappear and reappear somewhere else. But three weeks ago, Harvey had made contact with an attorney—Robert Marchetti. The nature of that contact remains unclear, but Marchetti's distress afterward suggested Harvey had asked for something dangerous: help in leaving.

## The Mercer Connection

By four o'clock I'm back at the Whitfield Building, taking the elevator up to Mercer & Associates. This time I don't ask politely. This time I walk into Mercer's office like I own it.

Mercer is alone, which is lucky. He looks up from his desk with the expression of a man who's been expecting trouble but hoped it would take longer.

"You know what happened to Claire Hollister," I say, sitting down without waiting to be asked.
