## The Arrest

By eleven p.m., there's a development that changes everything. The police find Claire Hollister's jewelry—a bracelet and a ring—in Sinclair's apartment. They were discovered during a search that wasn't authorized by the judge but was conducted anyway, which is a problem legally, but it's real evidence nonetheless.

The bracelet is gold with three small pearls, and the ring is a sapphire in a silver setting. Both were photographed, catalogued, and verified by Margaret Hollister as belonging to her daughter. There's also fiber evidence on them, fabric that matches clothing found in Sinclair's apartment.

It's not a smoking gun. It's not a confession. But it's the kind of circumstantial evidence that, combined with the financial records and the body itself, suggests that Sinclair had physical contact with Claire before she died.

By midnight, Sinclair's arrested at his apartment. He's brought to the police station for booking. He's read his rights. He's advised that he can have a lawyer present during questioning.

He requests his lawyer, which is the smart move. But the smart move just means the trial's going to be longer and more expensive. It doesn't mean he walks free.

What nobody knows at this moment is that James Harvey—the man we've been searching for—will never be found. His apartment is empty. His bank account is closed. The woman with red hair who met him at the bar has disappeared as well. Victor's sources confirm that Harvey made it out of the city two weeks ago, aided by someone from within the network. Kaslan made a calculation: a junior facilitator gone quietly was better than a junior facilitator talking to police. The exit was purchased with the painting's return and Harvey's absolute silence.
