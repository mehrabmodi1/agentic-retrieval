
"No," Eva said, and a slight smile crossed her face—not unkind, but the smile of a woman who had learned to play the long game, who understood that information was more valuable than rent. "I told him that the lease, it required two months' notice. Which was true. And I told him the apartment upstairs would be available in December, but not before. He seemed to relax when I said this. Not happy, you understand. But relieved. Like he had been given a reprieve from something."

"Did anyone visit him? Did you see anyone going to his apartment?"

"I see many people," Eva said. "This is a big building. But unusual visitors for Mr. Harvey?" She tapped her temple with one finger. "Once, I see a man who does not belong. Nice suit, good shoes, but the walk, you know? The walk of someone who does not walk much. Too stiff. Too deliberate. He goes up to three F—I see him in the hallway because the light, it is not very good on the stairs and I am always checking the lights. He stays maybe forty minutes. When he comes down, his face is different. Like he has given Mr. Harvey something very important, or taken something away."

"Do you have a description?"

"Fifties, maybe sixty. White hair, silver almost. Heavy in the middle. An accent, I think, but I did not hear him speak. But there is something about him that says he has traveled. The way he holds his body. The way he looks at things like he is cataloging them, like everything might be important later. A man does not move that way unless he has spent his life in dangerous places."

Chen wrote this down carefully. "When was this?"

"Three weeks ago. Maybe two weeks and five days. I remember because it was the day before I ordered new carpet for the downstairs hallway, and the man, he looked like he would notice the carpet. Like he would file away that we have worn burgundy carpet, so if he needed to report on the building to someone, he could say exactly what it looks like."

"Did Mr. Harvey seem different after this man's visit?"

Eva's hands tightened on her ledger. "He stopped pacing at night. Or maybe he paced quieter. And there was something in his eyes the next time I saw him—he came to pay rent, he always comes in person—something that said he had made a decision about something. A big decision. The kind that changes your life."

"When did you last see him?"

"October 29th. He brings the rent check, and he seems lighter, like a weight has been removed. We speak about the weather—it is turning cold—and he says he might spend Thanksgiving with family. Family! In three years, he has never mentioned family. I don't even know if he has family. But that day he mentions it like he is making a promise to himself. Then he leaves."

"And you haven't seen him since?"

"No. But I begin to worry when he does not come out, not for three days, not for four. So on the fourth day, I go up to the apartment. I have a master key—for emergencies, in case someone is sick, has died, has become a danger to themselves. I knock first, loud. No answer. I use the key. The apartment, it is empty. His clothes are gone. His furniture, most of it. He left behind only what could not be carried—the old sofa that was here when he moved in, the kitchenette, things like that. But his personal things, his papers, his life—it is all gone."

"Did he leave any forwarding address?"

"Nothing. He just... vanishes. Like he was never here. But I know he was here because I have the rent checks, and I have the memory of a man who was quiet and careful and afraid. Those things, you cannot erase."

When asked if anyone unusual had visited Mr. Harvey's apartment, Eva nodded slowly. "The heavy man, the one I mentioned before—Gregg, they called him—he came back three days before Mr. Harvey left. This time, I heard their conversation through the thin walls. Mr. Gregg said, 'Your situation has become unsustainable. Kaslan has made a decision.' Mr. Harvey said nothing at first, then asked, 'What decision?' The response was: 'The kind where you disappear completely, or you don't disappear at all.'"
